VPN to ZTNA: Animated Phased Journey

A VPN replacement moves people, not circuits — so it stalls the same way every WAN migration does: in the middle, with some users on the new client, some on the old, and contractors nobody wants to touch. This page is the animated companion to the full remote-access playbooks: press play and watch a concentrator estate retire one cohort at a time, from full-tunnel backhaul to always-on ZTNA with the headends switched off.

Business objective

Cohorts, not cutovers

Site migrations co-exist by routing; client migrations co-exist by cohort. At any moment mid-rollout some users run the legacy VPN agent and some run the Cato Client — but no machine runs both full-tunnel, and the headends stay warm as the rollback path until the last wave clears its soak window. That is the pattern the animation below plays out. For the full playbooks — discovery, posture mapping, MDM mechanics — see AnyConnect to Cato Client and DirectAccess to Cato SDP; for why per-app ZTNA beats network-wide VPN access for third parties, see De-risking 3rd-Party Supply Chain Access.

At each phase

What changes, and who connects how

PhaseWhat changesWho connects how
0 · Full tunnel Baseline — every employee and contractor full-tunnels to the datacentre concentrators, which must be sized for peak, patched on the vendor's schedule and left listening on the internet. Everyone on the VPN; one tunnel grants the whole network, and cloud-bound traffic hairpins via the DC.
1 · Pilot cohort The Cato side goes up: apps behind a datacentre Socket, IdP SSO + MFA connected, posture profiles in monitor. MDM swaps the pilot cohort — remove the VPN profile, install the Cato Client — in one change window. Pilot on ZTNA via the nearest PoP; everyone else still on the live headends.
2 · Cohort waves Departmental waves repeat the pilot's swap and acceptance checklist. Network-wide VPN grants are replaced by per-app least-privilege rules; rollback stays per-cohort and mechanical. Employees on Cato; contractors still on the VPN.
3 · Clientless access Contractors and BYOD move to clientless browser access — no agent to install on devices you don't manage, and no network address issued. The concentrators drain to standby, kept warm only as the rollback path. Everyone on Cato; headends idle through the final soak window.
4 · Headends off Tunnel-groups disabled, concentrators decommissioned, support renewals and client licences cancelled. Posture flips from monitor to enforce and always-on protects users before they think about connecting. Always-on ZTNA with continuous posture checks — and no internet-exposed headend left to patch.
Why nobody loses access mid-flight

Cohorts swap clients rather than stacking two full-tunnel agents on one machine, so each user is always on exactly one working path — and rollback is a per-cohort MDM change measured in minutes, cheap only for as long as the headends stay racked, licensed and monitored. Contractors never install anything at all, which is precisely why they move on the clientless wave. The mechanics — discovery, posture mapping, the helpdesk playbook — are in AnyConnect to Cato Client and DirectAccess to Cato SDP.
