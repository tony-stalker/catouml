Check Point (Quantum & Harmony) to Cato

A Check Point estate concentrates pressure in two calendars — the December 2025 appliance end-of-support wave and R81.10's March 2026 software deadline — and in a management-heavy operating model: SmartConsole layers, per-gateway blades, identity collectors and, since the Perimeter 81 acquisition, a second policy plane in Harmony SASE. This page maps Quantum, SmartConsole and Harmony to the Cato platform and lays out a phased migration in which every cutover is a route announcement — and every rollback a withdrawal.

Business objective

Why this matters — and why now

Nobody replaces a working Quantum estate on a whim. What puts this migration on the table is that Check Point's own lifecycle calendar is forcing spend on the status quo. A large cohort of widely deployed appliances — the 3100/3200, 5100–5900, 15400/15600 and 23500/23800/23900 series — reaches end of support in December 2025, so Quantum Force refresh quotes are landing now. Estates on R81.10 face its March 2026 end of support — a forced major-version upgrade project that must touch the management servers first, then every gateway. Either way, money and change windows are about to be spent; the honest question is whether to spend them renewing the appliance model or retiring it.

The second decision point is the operating model itself. Security value is sold as software blades enabled per gateway; policy lives in SmartConsole's ordered and inline layers; identity needs its own collector servers; and since the Perimeter 81 acquisition, Check Point's SASE answer — Harmony SASE — runs in its own portal application with its own agent and its own policy model, separate from the Quantum rulebase. Customers of the previous cloud service, Harmony Connect, have already been through one forced platform migration inside the same vendor (sk184452). The objective: retire the appliance and management estate on the customer's timetable, co-existing cleanly with Check Point throughout, with per-prefix rollback designed into every step.

The hardware EOS wave Dec 2025

Check Point's published support life cycle puts a broad set of branch and DC appliances out of support in December 2025. Refresh quotes for Quantum Force replacements arrive at exactly the moment a SASE alternative can be tabled — the spend is happening either way.

The software deadline R81.10 · Mar 2026

R80.40 went end of support in April 2024, R81 in October 2024, and R81.10 follows in March 2026. Staying current means a major-version project across the Security Management Server (first) and every gateway — recurring engineering effort that a cloud-delivered service simply removes.

One forced migration already sk184452

Harmony Connect — Check Point's first-generation cloud SSE — went end of sale at the end of 2023 with a formal EOL and service-termination notice. Its replacement, Harmony SASE, is the acquired Perimeter 81 stack (~$490m, completed September 2023): its own Infinity Portal application, agent and policy model, distinct from the Quantum rulebase. The "single platform" is in practice at least two policy planes.

The appliance is attack surface CVE-2024-24919

A zero-day information disclosure in internet-exposed Quantum gateways with Remote Access VPN or Mobile Access enabled was exploited in the wild from April 2024 and added to CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue. Hotfixes were required across R80.40–R81.20, Maestro, Scalable Chassis and Spark. On Cato, the PoPs are patched by Cato — there is no customer-side gateway hotfix cycle.

The Cato solution

Recommended migration path

Read this first

This page is authored from sourced research and the Cato Professional Services reference material — it is not a delivery-verified runbook. Review the plan with Cato Professional Services before using it with a customer.

The plan follows the Cato PS methodology — Discover & Design → Pilot, Build & Initial Rollout → Advanced Security & Broader Rollout → Tune, Optimise & Handover, across a typical four-month reference plan. Its heart is the parallel-socket pattern: the socket is installed alongside the Check Point edge, the Quantum gateway keeps the default gateway role, and a transit-VLAN handoff — static routes or eBGP (Gaia supports BGP natively) — moves subnets to Cato one prefix at a time. Cato-side routes are tagged with the Cato ASN and community 32768 so they can be isolated and filtered cleanly; for multi-site estates, an interconnecting hub site keeps legacy and Cato islands talking throughout.

Migrated — via the Cato PoP (socket / Cato Client) Legacy — via the Quantum gateway (Endpoint VPN)
Parallel build: the socket sits alongside the Quantum gateway, which keeps the default gateway role. The transit-VLAN handoff moves subnets to Cato one prefix at a time while user cohorts swap from Endpoint Security VPN (or the Harmony SASE agent) to the Cato Client in waves. Rollback at every step is a route withdrawal, not a rebuild.

  1. Export the estate and clean the rulebase

    Pull each Access Control layer with mgmt_cli show access-rulebase (or the Web API) using use-object-dictionary true, show-hits true and --format json. Two API realities to plan for: limit caps at 500 per call (paginate with offset), and each query returns a single layer — inline layers must be queried separately. The open-source ExportImportPolicyPackage tool exports a full policy package (R80.10+) for offline analysis. Capture NAT rules, Threat Prevention profiles, HTTPS Inspection policy, VPN communities, Identity Awareness sources and any Harmony SASE tenant configuration; on MDS, repeat per domain. Then clean before translating: use hit counts and last-modified metadata to strip zero-hit, disabled and shadowed rules and collapse duplicate objects — a decade of SmartConsole history translated verbatim just imports the mess.

  2. Build the parallel network

    Network → Sites

    Stand up sockets (or vSockets) alongside the Check Point estate with the Quantum gateway — or Maestro Security Group — still the L3 default gateway. Establish the transit-VLAN handoff per site: static routes or eBGP between socket and gateway, with Cato-side routes isolated by the Cato ASN and community 32768. Below roughly 20 sites, parallel connectivity at every site is the preferred pattern; at scale, add an interconnecting hub site (legacy CPE + socket) so legacy and Cato islands keep talking. Validate routing symmetry and return paths with test prefixes before any production move. Foundation work lands here too: CMA baseline, IdP/SCIM integration, SIEM export, and early distribution of the Cato Client and TLS root certificate.

  3. Swap user cohorts — SDP Migration Groups

    Access → Device Posture

    Run remote access as phased waves (the PS reference plan uses SDP Migration Groups 1–4 across months 2–4). Per cohort: enrol users via SCIM → enable the Cato Client → confirm application access → remove the legacy client — Endpoint Security VPN or the Harmony SASE agent — from that cohort's devices. Keep the swap short and scripted: third-party VPN drivers on the same machine can conflict with the Cato Client. Start Client Connectivity and Device Posture policy in monitor mode for device discovery, and tune between waves.

  4. Migrate sites in waves, one prefix at a time

    Security → TLS Inspection

    Move branches in groups; at each site's window, shift its subnets to the socket via the static or eBGP handoff rather than a big-bang LAN cutover — every prefix is individually revertible by withdrawing the route. Re-home anything DNAT-published through the Check Point NAT rulebase to Remote Port Forwarding (Cato allocated IPs) or Local Port Forwarding, update external DNS, and validate the return path before the gateway is retired. In parallel, rebuild HTTPS Inspection as phased Cato TLSi — block QUIC, certificate deployed, then gradual enablement from high-risk/low-impact categories outward — and bring IPS/NGAM through monitor-then-block, with CASB discovery and DLP in monitor mode once the baseline is stable.

  5. Decommission — management goes last

    Retire in dependency order: the legacy remote-access surface (Remote Access VPN / Mobile Access blades, Harmony SASE tenant) once the final cohort has migrated → branch gateways → DC gateways, Maestro Security Groups and orchestrators once traffic and events verify clean → Identity Collector servers → the Security Management Server / MDS last, only after SmartLog/SmartEvent data is exported or archived to satisfy retention obligations. Terminate blade subscriptions and support contracts at renewal.

Co-existence & rollback

Running both networks without breaking either

Placement is decided per site class in discovery: an L3 core switch can own the routing decision (keeping interim throughput off the Quantum gateway), the Check Point can stay as default gateway with the transit-VLAN handoff (the conservative default shown above), or the socket becomes default gateway with Alt-WAN / LAN BGP routing not-yet-migrated traffic back to the legacy network. Whichever pattern is chosen, every phase keeps a designed rollback lever:

PhaseRollback lever
Pilot siteRemove the static route or withdraw the BGP prefix at the switch or gateway — traffic reverts to Check Point instantly
Subnet migration (routed-range pattern)Re-add the dummy range on the Cato site and restore the statics at the interconnect — the designed rollback
BGP handoffWithdraw the prefix; routes propagate back automatically
User cohortsRe-enable the legacy client for the affected cohort — keep the VPN blades / Harmony SASE tenant warm until the final wave completes
TLS inspectionRoll back to an earlier enablement phase without disabling TLSi entirely
Full sitePhysical LAN cutover reversal — big-bang cutovers rate as “complex rollback”, which is why the parallel patterns are preferred
The big one: asymmetric routing through a stateful Check Point

If traffic egresses via Cato but returns via the Quantum gateway — or crosses regions through different hubs — Check Point's stateful inspection drops the unseen flow. ClusterXL and Maestro flow ownership make it strict about this. Design it out with parallel connectivity per region, hub anchoring, or AS-path design that keeps regional symmetry — and validate return paths before every cutover.

Double TLS inspection

If the HTTPS Inspection blade stays enabled on a path Cato now also inspects, users hit certificate-on-certificate failures and pinned apps break twice over. Disable gateway HTTPS Inspection for Cato-routed traffic, or scope Cato TLSi to bypass those paths until cutover.

Dual tunnel clients

Cato documents that third-party VPN drivers on the same machine can conflict with and override Cato Client settings. Keep the per-cohort swap short and scripted — install Cato Client → validate → uninstall Endpoint Security VPN or the Harmony SASE agent — and never leave two tunnel clients active long-term.

Inbound published services

Anything DNAT-published through the Check Point NAT rulebase must be consciously re-homed to Remote Port Forwarding (Cato allocated IPs) or Local Port Forwarding — with external DNS updated and the return route validated — before the gateway is retired. These move on their own change windows.

Identity continuity

Rules that depend on Identity Awareness (PDP/PEP-shared identities) need their Cato-side equivalents proven — SCIM/LDAP sync populated, Identity Agent deployed where non-Client users must be identified — before any gateway-side identity source is switched off.

Gotchas & objections

What will come up in the room

Layer semantics do not translate mechanically

In Check Point, an Accept in one Ordered Layer continues matching into the next (every layer must pass), each layer carries its own implicit cleanup action, Inline Layers nest parent/sub rules, and layers can be shared and delegated across policy packages. Cato's WAN/Internet/LAN firewalls are flat, first-match rulebases with fixed default postures. Model the effective policy — what actually passes all layers — not the layer structure, and budget conversion effort by layers and inter-layer dependencies, not raw rule count.

NAT lives in its own rulebase

Access policy and NAT policy are separate in SmartConsole, with automatic (object-driven) and manual NAT rules evaluated first-match. Inventory both types during export. Most branch hide-NAT simply disappears behind the PoP; DC manual NAT — static NAT, port translation, no-NAT exemptions between internal networks — needs line-by-line disposition against Cato's site-level NAT and RPF/LPF model.

Identity Awareness has infrastructure of its own

AD Query needs privileged WMI access to domain controllers; Identity Collector runs on Windows servers polling identity sources; PDP gateways share identities with PEP enforcement points. These are components to inventory and decommission — and rules that silently depend on identity (including in HTTPS Inspection and Application Control layers) must be flagged in discovery. Verify Cato licence coverage for every user the policy must identify.

“We need Maestro-class hyperscale”

Maestro load-shares up to 52 appliances behind orchestrators as one logical gateway — it solves an appliance scaling problem Cato does not have, because inspection capacity lives in the PoPs. The honest counter: DC egress/WAN throughput becomes a socket/vSocket sizing and HA design conversation, and the Maestro-specific operational surface — MHO firmware, SGM membership, Security Group design, dual-site sync — disappears entirely. Size formally; never quote throughput figures from memory.

VSX estates need a target tenancy model

Each Virtual System has its own interfaces, routing and policy. A single Cato account with scoped WAN FW / LAN FW rules usually replaces context separation; genuine multi-tenant requirements (divested units, regulated separation) may justify separate accounts. The hard case is overlapping IP space across Virtual Systems — surface it in week one, because it forces re-addressing or NAT design either way.

“Check Point has the best threat prevention”

Efficacy debates rarely land — anchor on operational reality instead. Blade-based protections are bounded by the appliance's sizing and software train; HTTPS Inspection is commonly constrained or switched off on appliances for performance and complexity reasons (Check Point maintains a dedicated best-practice SK for it); and CVE-2024-24919 showed the gateway itself is attack surface. Cato delivers IPS, NGAM, sandboxing and TLSi at every PoP, always current, with no customer patch cycle.

“We already own Harmony SASE”

It is a separate stack — the Perimeter 81-derived cloud, with its own agent and portal application, distinct from the Quantum rulebase. The estate still runs two policy planes and two inspection paths, and Harmony Connect customers have already been through one forced platform migration inside the same vendor (sk184452). Argue consoles-and-policies, not feature checklists.

Timing leverage

The December 2025 hardware EOS wave and the R81.10 March 2026 software deadline both force spend on the status quo. Align the business case and pilot to land before the customer signs a Quantum Force refresh or an R81.20/R82 upgrade project — after that, the window closes for years.
