Fortinet (FortiGate & FortiSASE) to Cato

Fortinet estates are usually all-in: the same FortiGate at every site is the firewall, the UTM stack and the SD-WAN edge, held together by FortiManager, FortiAnalyzer and FortiClient EMS. This page maps that estate to the Cato SASE Cloud and lays out a staged migration built on a proven co-existence pattern — a parallel socket at each FortiGate regional hub with an eBGP handoff — so every cutover step has a rollback that is just BGP reconverging.

Business objective

Why this matters — and why now

Nobody replaces a working FortiGate estate on a whim. What puts this migration on the table is the convergence of three calendars the customer does not control: the patch treadmill (FortiOS is the most-listed product in CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalogue, and emergency SSL-VPN patches keep landing at awkward times), the hardware refresh cycle (End of Support typically falls 60 months after End of Order, and support cannot be renewed past it — so every refresh re-opens the box-versus-cloud decision), and now a forced remote-access re-engineering: from FortiOS 7.6.3, SSL-VPN tunnel mode is removed from GUI and CLI, and existing SSL-VPN configuration is not upgraded.

That last one is the natural decision point. If the customer must re-engineer remote access anyway, the honest question is whether to rebuild it on the same appliance stack — with the same patching burden and refresh cycle — or move it, and eventually the sites behind it, to a cloud-delivered platform that Cato patches and scales for them. The objective: retire the appliance estate on the customer's timetable, co-existing cleanly with FortiGate SD-WAN throughout, with rollback designed into every step.

The management plane was the breach FortiJump

CVE-2024-47575 (CVSS 9.8) — missing authentication in FortiManager's FGFM daemon — was exploited in the wild from June 2024. Mandiant investigated 50+ compromised appliances from which attackers exfiltrated the configurations and hashed credentials of every managed FortiGate. The single pane of glass became a single point of compromise.

Patching doesn't always end it CISA, Apr 2025

CISA relayed a Fortinet advisory on a post-exploitation technique: attackers left symbolic links on FortiGates that preserved read-only access after the original vulnerability was patched. The remediation is full device re-validation, not just an upgrade — a material operational cost per incident. CISA alerted again in January 2026 on active exploitation of the FortiOS authentication bypass CVE-2026-24858.

SSL-VPN removal forces rework FortiOS 7.6.3

SSL-VPN tunnel mode is removed from GUI and CLI and replaced with IPsec; existing SSL-VPN tunnel configuration and its firewall policies are not upgraded. Remote access must be re-engineered either way — the migration effort is being spent whether or not Cato is in the picture.

Refresh and licence stacking Lifecycle

Hardware EOS typically lands 60 months after End of Order, with the last service extension 12 months before it. Security value is licensed per appliance — FortiCare plus a FortiGuard bundle (ATP / UTP / Enterprise Protection), stacked on FortiManager, FortiAnalyzer, EMS and, separately, FortiSASE. Every refresh means re-sizing boxes and licences.

Questions worth asking

“Which FortiGates are inside 18 months of End of Support? What did the last emergency FortiOS patch cycle cost you in change windows and re-validation? And if you use SSL-VPN today — what's the plan for 7.6.3?”

The Cato solution

Recommended migration path

Read this first

This page is authored from sourced research and the Cato Professional Services reference material — it is not a delivery-verified runbook. Review the plan with Cato Professional Services before using it with a customer.

The plan is anchored to the Cato PS methodology (typical reference plan: four months). Its heart is the regional-hub co-existence pattern, demonstrated by Cato PS against a live FortiGate Secure SD-WAN estate: a parallel socket at each FortiGate regional hub, an eBGP handoff between the two domains, and AS-path length anchoring traffic to its local hub so paths stay symmetric — no broken stateful inspection on the FortiGates, no per-site route engineering during the window.

Migrated — Cato socket via nearest PoP Legacy — FortiGate ADVPN overlay
Interim co-existence: the parallel socket at each FortiGate regional hub exchanges routes over eBGP. Routes crossing regions carry an extra AS hop, so best-path selection anchors traffic to its local hub and paths stay symmetric. Spoke cutover: disable the FortiGate spoke → bring up the Cato site → routes propagate to both domains. Rollback is the mirror image.

Guard-rails from the PS design: filter the default route and the prefixes of any site with parallel connectivity at each handoff, so a half-migrated site is never reachable via two paths at once; use BGP summaries and Cato's BGP filtering to keep the exchange tidy. The Cato side is eBGP-only (default Cato ASN 64515). Where no socket can be placed at a hub, Cato publishes a FortiGate HA IPsec tunnel design as the handoff instead.

  1. Discover & design (weeks 1–4)

    Export every FortiGate config (execute backup full-config) or pull configs and policy packages from FortiManager; mine FortiAnalyzer / FortiView for the real traffic matrix — ADVPN shortcuts mean spoke-to-spoke flows rarely appear in any design document. Clean the rulebase before translating it: use hit counts to retire dead rules, collapse duplicate objects, flag any-any debt. Classify the estate — regional hubs, DCs, branch classes, VDOM tenants, inbound NAT/VIP services, and everything hanging off FortiLink. Agree the co-existence design: AS numbering, route filtering and summarisation, and the cutover template per site class. Stand up the foundation: CMA baseline, IdP/SCIM, client and TLS-inspection certificate readiness, socket logistics.

  2. Build the parallel hubs (month 2)

    Deploy sockets (or vSockets in AWS/Azure) at the data centres and at each FortiGate regional hub, alongside the existing kit. Establish the eBGP handoffs, then validate routing symmetry and failover with test prefixes before any production site moves. Nothing about the legacy estate changes in this phase — that is the point.

  3. Convert policy & run the pilot

    Security → WAN Firewall Security → Internet Firewall

    Translate the cleaned rulebase: WAN Firewall for site-to-site and east-west intent, the wizard-assisted Internet Firewall for the initial egress policy, IPS and Anti-Malware in monitor-then-block. Recreate SD-WAN steering intent (voice, business-critical, bulk) as network rules with bandwidth profiles. Cut over one representative branch using the spoke methodology below, and put a pilot user cohort on the Cato Client with Client Connectivity Policy in monitor mode. The pilot's rollback is the standard spoke rollback — designed in, not bolted on.

  4. Migrate in waves (month 3)

    Access → Device Posture

    Branches move region by region behind their hub handoff, each following the same four moves: disable the FortiGate spoke (its prefixes are withdrawn from the overlay and the eBGP exchange) → bring up the Cato socket site → routes propagate to both domains automatically → rollback, if needed, is re-enabling the spoke. Keep waves small early, then batch: simple single-WAN branches → HA branches → sites with local inbound services → hubs last. In parallel, migrate FortiClient to the Cato Client in cohorts — disable FortiClient VPN/ZTNA via EMS per wave, enable the Cato Client, re-point posture enforcement — and phase TLS inspection in by URL category and pilot group, then CASB and DLP monitor-mode → enforcement.

  5. Tune, optimise & decommission (month 4)

    Final site and user migrations; QoS and policy tuning from CMA analytics; security-control validation. Remove each region's eBGP handoff once it is fully migrated, retire the FortiGate hubs, and harvest FortiCare / FortiGuard renewals against the EOS calendar. Decommission FortiManager, FortiAnalyzer and EMS only once log-retention obligations are met. Handover, admin training, SIEM integration live.

Co-existence & rollback

Branch patterns and the transition estate

Not every site fits one mould. The PS deck's branch-level variants below are chosen per site class in Phase 1 — start conservative (FortiGate keeps the default gateway) and reserve socket-as-gateway for DC-class sites that need full HA. Where BGP is unavailable, the static alternative is an interconnect site with routed ranges: pre-stage real subnets on the interconnect and dummy ranges on the future Cato sites, then swap at cutover — rollback is re-adding the statics.

PatternDefault gatewayMechanicsRollback
FortiGate stays DG, static routes FortiGate Socket on a LAN/DMZ transit; FortiGate adds specific routes to the socket per migrated subnet Remove the statics — traffic reverts to the SD-WAN overlay instantly
FortiGate stays DG, eBGP over LAN transit FortiGate eBGP peering socket ↔ FortiGate; phased subnet migration with dynamic propagation Withdraw advertisements or drop the peering
L3 switch decides L3 switch Static or BGP routing between legacy and migrated subnets on the core switch; keeps interim throughput off the firewall Flip routes on the switch
Socket as DG, eBGP LAN handoff Cato Socket Socket is the gateway with full HA support; eBGP handoff for legacy prefixes — suits DC-class sites Re-point the default gateway to the FortiGate
Socket as DG, Alt-WAN BGP / Gradual Deployment Cato Socket Legacy overlay reached via Alt-WAN with eBGP, or Gradual Deployment routing unknown RFC1918 via Alt-WAN Routes revert via BGP — see caveats below
Alt-WAN caveats (from the PS deck)

Configure local routing for all traffic so Alt-WAN-destined flows are not first carried to the PoP (avoiding asymmetry), and note that Alt-WAN failure does not trigger socket HA failover — prefer BGP on a LAN handoff where HA matters. Gradual Deployment covers private (RFC1918) destinations only.

FortiClient → Cato Client, in waves

The PS reference plan uses four SDP migration waves. Per cohort: disable FortiClient VPN/ZTNA via EMS, enable the Cato Client, re-point posture enforcement. Keep exactly one VPN/ZTNA data path active per endpoint; retain FortiClient's EPP function temporarily if it is also the endpoint AV.

Phased TLS inspection

Enable TLS inspection by URL category and/or pilot group — the PS reference is four phases over four weeks — then layer CASB discovery, tenant controls and DLP in monitor mode before enforcement. Certificate deployment happens in Phase 1, not at enablement time.

Re-home FortiLink first

Where FortiSwitches and FortiAPs are FortiLink-managed, the branch FortiGate is also the LAN and Wi-Fi controller. Move switching and Wi-Fi to standalone or cloud management before the gate is decommissioned — build it into the site survey.

Inbound services deliberately

Published services (FortiGate VIPs) move on their own change windows: re-publish via Cato (remote port forwarding / static-IP egress) or leave them on a retained FortiGate leg until their window. East-west enforcement can stay on a retained FortiGate as an interim LAN firewall, or move to the Socket LAN Firewall policy.

Gotchas & objections

What will come up in the room

“FortiGate gives us hardware-accelerated line rate”

The datasheet headline is ASIC-accelerated firewall throughput; Fortinet's own product matrix shows threat protection and SSL inspection at a fraction of that figure — and the fleet must be sized to the inspected number, per box, at every refresh. Cato decrypts and inspects once, in parallel engines at the PoP, and capacity is the platform's problem. WAN and internet traffic is bounded by the access link, not the socket; for genuine local line-rate needs, keep east-west enforcement on the LAN.

“We've invested years in FortiManager automation”

Acknowledge it — Jinja templates, scripts and policy packages are real sunk effort. But most of that automation exists to keep hundreds of boxes consistent, a problem class that disappears with a single cloud rulebase. What remains (site onboarding, policy-as-code) maps to the GraphQL API and Terraform provider. The sharper point, made factually: FortiJump showed the management plane itself can be the breach vector, exposing every managed device's config and credentials.

“ZTNA tags handle posture-based access today”

EMS tagging rules sync to FortiGates as dynamic address objects, and there is no automated converter. Inventory the tagging rules, keep only those actually referenced in policy, and re-model them as Cato device posture profiles plus Client Connectivity and access policy. Fortinet's own SSL-VPN-to-ZTNA path already forces this rework on 7.6.3+ — the effort is spent either way.

“We'll just add FortiSASE instead”

FortiSASE covers remote users and secure internet access, but private access (SPA) using ZTNA is delivered through FortiGate hubs acting as application gateways — the appliance estate, its refresh cycle and its patching burden stay in the critical path, and branches still need FortiGate hardware. It is an addition to the stack, with its own subscription, not a consolidation of it.

SD-WAN rules are deeply tied to FortiOS

True — SD-WAN rules reference performance SLAs, zones and ISDB objects, and ADVPN behaviour is emergent rather than documented. Treat translation as design work, not lift-and-shift: recover intent and express it as network rules plus bandwidth profiles. Budget discovery time for the ADVPN traffic matrix.

VDOM tenancy needs a target model

Multi-VDOM estates (shared services, M&A tenants, MSSP-style separation) need an explicit decision: policy sections and site scoping within one account, or separate Cato accounts for hard separation. Flag it in week one — it shapes the whole rulebase design.

Feature-parity edge cases

Niche FortiOS behaviours — proxy-mode inspection specifics, video filtering, bespoke NAT constructs, local-in policies — should be listed in discovery and validated in the PoV rather than assumed. Standard PoV hygiene.
