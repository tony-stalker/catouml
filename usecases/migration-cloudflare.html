Cloudflare (Zero Trust & Magic WAN) to Cato

Cloudflare One estates are rarely designed — they accrete. The CDN lands first; then one team puts a wiki behind Access, another spins up a cloudflared tunnel, DNS filtering switches on for roaming laptops, and one day the customer has an accidental SASE with thin branch networking underneath it. This page maps Access, Gateway, WARP, cloudflared and Magic WAN to their Cato equivalents and walks a five-phase migration — cohort-based client swap, site re-termination onto Sockets, connector retirement app-by-app — while the CDN, WAF, authoritative DNS and Magic Transit stay exactly where they are: on Cloudflare.

Business objective

Why this matters

Research-based page — not a delivery-verified runbook

This plan is authored from Cloudflare and Cato documentation plus the Cato PS migration methodology — not from a completed, referenceable Cloudflare migration. Review it with Cato Professional Services before using it with a customer, especially the Access policy mapping scope, the service-token inventory and the egress-IP re-keying plan.

Cloudflare almost always lands in an account through the CDN / WAF / DNS side — the website, the API, the DDoS story — and Zero Trust is adopted afterwards, app-by-app. The commercial model encourages exactly this: a genuinely free Zero Trust plan for up to 50 users, then self-serve pay-as-you-go, then a negotiated Enterprise contract. What discovery finds is rarely a designed SASE estate — it is an accretion of per-feature configuration across Access, Gateway, Tunnels and Magic WAN, sometimes split across teams and even across Cloudflare accounts.

The "free / cheap" perception is set by the entry tiers, but the capabilities an enterprise actually needs sit in add-ons and contracts: Browser Isolation is a paid add-on (pay-as-you-go and Enterprise plans); dedicated egress IPs are an Enterprise add-on; custom DLP, long log retention and SIEM integrations sit in the Enterprise contract; Magic WAN is a separately purchased network service. Get the customer's real invoice on the table before any price debate.

Branch networking is the thin end of the estate. Magic WAN connects sites via anycast GRE or IPsec tunnels from the customer's existing routers and firewalls; Cloudflare does now ship a lightweight box — the Magic WAN Connector, renamed the Cloudflare One Appliance — so "no appliance at all" is not a fair line. Be accurate about what its own reference documentation shows instead: ICMP is routed to the internet and bypasses Gateway inspection, there is no fail-open mode, HA failover can take up to 30 seconds, and the reference documents no QoS or bandwidth-management capability — priority steering, not shaping. There is no published last-mile management service either; the ISP relationship stays the customer's problem.

On analyst positioning: in the 2025 Gartner Magic Quadrant for SASE Platforms, Cato is a Leader (repeat placement) and Cloudflare is a Visionary — with approximately 400 active enterprise SASE customers per Gartner's estimate (attribute that figure to Gartner; it is not a Cloudflare statement). Honest framing for the room: Cloudflare's network and web-facing ZTNA are genuinely strong. The gap is SASE platform maturity on the WAN/branch side and enterprise operational depth. Sell the platform difference, not FUD about their network.

Scope: downgrade the tenant — don't close it

This migration covers Zero Trust and WAN only: Access, Gateway, WARP, cloudflared and Magic WAN. Cato does not replace Cloudflare's CDN, WAF, authoritative DNS, Workers or Magic Transit — and should not try. Write a stays-on-Cloudflare register in phase 1 (CDN/WAF zones, DNS, Workers, Magic Transit prefixes, email security) and agree it with the customer: it defuses the "you're asking us to leave Cloudflare" objection early. The end-state is a live Cloudflare tenant serving the web estate with its Zero Trust seats reduced — the subscription is downgraded, never the account deleted.

Ask yourself

"Could you produce, today, a complete list of your Access applications, service tokens, cloudflared tunnels and dedicated egress IPs — and say which Cloudflare account and plan tier each one lives in?" Fragmented, multi-account Zero Trust estates are the norm, not the exception — and the service tokens and egress-IP allowlists are the items that break silently mid-migration if they are not found here.

Component mapping

What maps to what

Every Zero Trust and WAN function has a Cato home — and two rows deliberately say "stays on Cloudflare". Two mappings change shape rather than name: Access policies become firewall rules against app segments (rebuild the intent, don't transliterate rule-for-rule), and cloudflared's per-app daemons are replaced by site-level connectivity — one Socket typically replaces several connectors at once.

Recommended migration path

Five phases, cohort by cohort

Anchored to the Cato PS methodology — Discover & Design → Pilot, Build & Initial Rollout → Advanced Security & Broader Rollout → Tune & Optimise — with waved, business-unit-based SDP migration groups and phased TLS inspection. The unit of migration is the cohort: a user wave, a site, or an application, each with its own rollback lever.

  1. Discover & design — export the estate, write the stays-on-Cloudflare register

    Inventory precisely; the estate is usually more fragmented than the customer believes. Access: every application and its include/require/exclude policies, IdP integrations (often several), service tokens (find the machine consumers — they break silently), posture rules tied to WARP. Gateway: DNS policies and DNS locations, HTTP/network policies, TLS-inspection state and the root-certificate deployment mechanism, Do-Not-Inspect lists. Connectivity: every cloudflared connector (host, replicas, which apps/networks ride it), every Magic WAN tunnel and any Cloudflare One Appliance, the WARP split-tunnel configuration. Egress: third-party SaaS allowlists keyed to dedicated egress IPs.

    If the customer manages Cloudflare as code, the Terraform repo is the inventory; if not, use Cloudflare's cf-terraforming tool to export existing resources to HCL — it doubles as a point-in-time configuration snapshot for the runbook. Write and agree the stays-on-Cloudflare register (CDN/WAF, authoritative DNS, Workers, Magic Transit, email security). Stand up the Cato foundation in parallel: IdP/SSO + SCIM, PoP/region plan, Socket logistics, TLS-inspection certificate readiness. Design decisions: Socket vs IPsec per site; Client-based ZTNA vs Browser Access per app; TLS-inspection wave plan by category; Cato allocated egress IPs per region.

  2. Pilot — parallel Socket site, then the WARP → Cato Client cohort swap

    Access → Client Connectivity Policy Monitor → Experience Monitoring

    Deploy a Socket at one representative branch (or IPsec from the existing router if hardware hasn't shipped) and validate internet and WAN paths in parallel with Magic WAN, which stays up untouched. Convert the SWG baseline from Cato's recommended policies, then the customer's Gateway intent; convert Access apps to ZTNA rules for the pilot's applications.

    For pilot users, treat WARP and the Cato Client as mutually exclusive on a device — never dual-agent. Cloudflare's own guidance describes its client and any other VPN agent as competing for routing, DNS and firewall control; Cato documents client coexistence limits too. The clean pattern: per cohort, disable/remove the WARP profile via MDM, then deploy the Cato Client — same maintenance window, per-device, reversible. Use pilot user groups in the Client Upgrade Policy to control version rollout, and capture a DEM baseline plus per-app access parity before widening.

  3. Roll out — BU waves, Magic WAN re-termination, connector retirement, DNS last

    Security → Internet Firewall Security → TLS Inspection

    Users: waved, business-unit-based SDP migration groups with posture analysis and Client Connectivity Policy tuning per wave. Sites: re-terminate Magic WAN sites one at a time — a Socket per site as the end-state, or an interim IPsec IKEv2 tunnel from the existing edge (primary + secondary tunnels to different Cato PoP IPs, per Cato's HA best practice). Private apps: as each app's ZTNA rules or Browser Access publication is validated, retire its cloudflared connector — one Socket typically covers several connectors, but sequence retirement app-by-app so rollback stays granular. DNS: roaming users move automatically with the client swap; re-point office DNS locations only after the site's traffic is on Cato — never leave a site's DNS on Cloudflare once its traffic path has moved, or policy and logging split across two vendors. Phase TLS inspection by URL category; run CASB and DLP in monitor mode before enforcing; replicate Browser Isolation intent as RBI "Isolate" actions on risky/uncategorised categories.

  4. Co-exist deliberately — per-app parallel ZTNA, per-layer rollback

    An Access application and its Cato ZTNA rules can be live simultaneously — pilot users reach the app via Cato while everyone else still comes through Access, and the cloudflared connector keeps serving Access until the last cohort moves. Cutover is per-app and per-cohort, never big-bang. Keep the WARP MDM profile and Zero Trust enrolment intact (not deleted), Magic WAN tunnel configurations idle but in place, and old egress IPs allowed in third-party SaaS allowlists until each wave completes. Details and levers in the next section.

  5. Optimise, then retire in order — and downgrade the tenant

    Network → Bandwidth Management Monitor → Events

    QoS and bandwidth-management profiles per site, policy fine-tuning from analytics, documentation, admin training, operational handover. Then decommission in dependency order: cloudflared connectors → Magic WAN tunnels/appliances → Gateway policies and DNS locations → Access applications → WARP/MDM artefacts → reduce Zero Trust seats. Do not delete the Cloudflare account: CDN, WAF, authoritative DNS — and Magic Transit, if present — remain live. The Zero Trust subscription is downgraded, not the tenant closed.

Co-existence & rollback

One of the safer co-existence stories

Cloudflare-to-Cato co-existence is comparatively low-drama because the two control planes barely touch: the retained Cloudflare services (CDN, WAF, DNS, Magic Transit) sit in front of public traffic, while Cato takes over the users and sites behind them. The one hard rule is the endpoint: swap the agent, never overlap it — both vendors document the routing/DNS/firewall contention when two full-tunnel clients share a device.

Interim — via Cloudflare Zero Trust (retiring) Target — via Cato PoP and backbone Retained — CDN / WAF / DNS / Magic Transit stay
Scoped migration: user cohorts swap agents one wave at a time, the site runs Magic WAN and Socket paths in parallel, and private apps move from cloudflared to SDP app-by-app — while the public web estate never leaves Cloudflare.

Rollback levers, per layer

💻

Users — one device operation

Re-enable the WARP (Cloudflare One Client) profile via MDM and remove the Cato Client; the user is back on Cloudflare in one push. Keep the WARP MDM profile and Zero Trust enrolment intact for the whole migration window.

🏢

Sites — re-point the routing

Magic WAN tunnel configurations (and any Cloudflare One Appliance) stay in place but idle until phase 5; re-pointing routing back to the existing GRE/IPsec tunnels restores the old path without re-provisioning.

🔌

Private apps — parallel ZTNA

The Access app and its cloudflared connector keep serving non-migrated cohorts while Cato serves the pilot. Retiring connectors app-by-app keeps the rollback unit small even when one Socket replaced several.

🌐

DNS — re-point per site

Office DNS rolls back by re-pointing to Cloudflare resolver/DoH endpoints; roaming users roll back automatically with the agent swap, which carries DNS protection with it in both directions.

What never moves

The Cloudflare tenant stays for CDN, WAF, authoritative DNS, Workers and Magic Transit. Public-facing services keep their Cloudflare proxy and WAF in front; Cato secures the users and sites behind them. Position this as an architectural division of labour — not a partial failure of the migration.

Gotchas & objection handling

Where to be careful — and honest

"Cloudflare is basically free"

True at the entry tiers (free ≤50 users, self-serve pay-as-you-go) — and that anchor must be defused. The enterprise comparison includes the add-ons (Browser Isolation, dedicated egress IPs), Enterprise contract features (custom DLP, retention, SIEM), Magic WAN, the appliance hardware and the operational cost of the DIY glue at branches. Compare invoices and run the TCO on the converged estate, never on list per-seat price.

Dual agents — never "test alongside"

Do not let anyone trial Cato beside WARP on the same laptop. Cloudflare's own docs describe its client and any other VPN agent as competing for routing, DNS and firewall control with responsibilities split per product; Cato documents client coexistence limits in "Preparing to Install the Cato Client". Cohort swap via MDM, one agent at a time — same window, reversible.

Config-as-code parity

Cloudflare's developer-first customers often manage everything in Terraform and will ask if they lose that. They don't: Cato has a public Terraform provider (catonetworks/cato — Socket sites, IPsec sites, WAN firewall rules, routing, identity) plus a GraphQL API and certified modules for bulk sites and vSockets. Be honest about the caveat: the API executes sequentially, so Cato recommends running Terraform with parallelism=1. Use cf-terraforming on the Cloudflare side as the export.

Egress-IP allowlists

Enterprise customers on dedicated egress IPs (including BYOIP egress) typically have third-party SaaS allowlists keyed to those IPs. Every one must be re-keyed to the customer's Cato allocated IPs — per app, during that app's cutover window, with the old Cloudflare IPs left allowed until the wave completes. Find them in phase 1; they are the most common cause of "it worked yesterday" tickets.

No appliance to swap

Many Cloudflare Zero Trust sites never had an edge device — a cloudflared daemon on a VM and WARP on laptops was the whole footprint. A Socket is therefore an addition to the site, not a swap. Where no hardware is wanted, use a vSocket (cloud) or IPsec from existing kit, or serve small sites Client-only. Frame the Socket as what makes the site a first-class WAN citizen — QoS, HA, last-mile visibility, ILMM eligibility — not an imposed box.

"Cloudflare has the bigger network"

Concede PoP count gracefully — Gartner's 2025 MQ summaries credit Cloudflare's network coverage and innovation. Pivot to what the SASE decision turns on: a Leader-placed platform, an SD-WAN edge with QoS and a managed last mile, and one policy engine for WAN + security — the areas where the same report places Cloudflare as a Visionary with a much smaller SASE base (per Gartner's ~400-customer estimate, attributed).

Naming churn in discovery

Cloudflare renamed components across 2024–2026 and customers mix the names freely: WARP is the "Cloudflare One Client", Magic WAN docs sit under "Cloudflare WAN", the Magic WAN Connector became the "Cloudflare One Appliance", and the suite has been Teams → Zero Trust → Cloudflare One. Inventory by capability, not by the brand name the customer remembers.

Access service tokens

Machine-to-machine flows authenticated by Access service tokens do not map one-to-one onto user-centric ZTNA. Identify every consumer in phase 1 and design each flow explicitly — a site-to-site path or an IP-scoped firewall rule — before its Access application is retired. These are the flows that break silently.

Positioning discipline

Use the documented gaps accurately and stop there: ICMP bypassing Gateway from the Cloudflare One Appliance, no fail-open, up-to-30-second failover, no QoS in the Connector reference, and the add-on SKUs — all sourced from Cloudflare's own docs. Do not extrapolate beyond them, and do not attack the Cloudflare network itself: their global edge and web-facing ZTNA are genuinely strong. The differential is platform convergence, the SD-WAN edge and enterprise operational depth.
