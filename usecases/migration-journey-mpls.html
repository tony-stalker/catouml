Business objective

The middle is where migrations stall

A WAN migration is never a single weekend. Circuits expire on different dates, so for months some sites sit on Cato while others are still on MPLS — and the only question that matters is whether they can all still talk to each other. They can, throughout. The walkthrough below steps through the five phases of a typical MPLS exit; for the routing tables line by line, see the full detail in MPLS to SASE Migration & Co-existence.

The walkthrough

The MPLS exit, phase by phase

Three branches — Bristol, Leeds and Glasgow — plus the London DC start hub-and-spoke on MPLS. At each phase

What changes, and who can reach whom

PhaseWhat changesReachability during this phase
0 · Legacy MPLS Baseline — every branch and the London DC backhaul hub-and-spoke through the MPLS cloud. Full any-to-any over MPLS; nothing on Cato yet.
1 · DC socket in London DC gains a Cato Socket and the backbone appears. The socket advertises the branch prefixes into Cato. Unchanged — branches still on MPLS; the DC is now reachable both ways.
2 · First site migrates Bristol cuts over to Cato; its MPLS last mile goes dormant. Bristol reaches the DC on-net, and Leeds/Glasgow via the DC bridge.
3 · Most sites moved Leeds cuts over too; the MPLS cloud shrinks to a single remaining site. Migrated sites are on-net; Glasgow still reaches everyone via the DC.
4 · MPLS retired Glasgow cuts over; the MPLS CE router and the contract are decommissioned. Full any-to-any over the Cato backbone; one routing table.
Why reachability never breaks

The London DC socket advertises the prefixes of every site still on MPLS into the Cato backbone — a static route pointed at the MPLS CE router on the DC LAN. So a migrated site always has a route to an un-migrated one (via the DC), and vice-versa. No site is ever cut off, at any point in the migration. The full co-existence use case walks the two routing tables that make this work, line by line.
