Versa Policy Migration

The network cutover gets the attention, but policy translation is where Versa migrations stall: VOS policy is generated from Director template stacks, its UTM lives in per-rule profiles, and in carrier-managed estates even the export is a change ticket. This page works the estate domain by domain — firewall rules and attached UTM, URL filtering, then CASB and DLP as greenfield best-practice builds — through one pipeline: export, rationalise on hit counts, map to CMA constructs, deploy in monitor mode, enforce on evidence.

Business objective

Policy translation is where migrations stall

Sockets ship, tunnels come up, BGP converges — and then the project sits for weeks while somebody untangles a decade of firewall rules. Policy is the long pole in almost every SD-WAN and security migration, and a Versa estate adds two twists of its own: the effective policy of any branch is the product of Director template layers (workflows, staging and post-staging templates, device templates, per-device bind data), and in carrier-managed estates the enterprise cannot simply export it — config, templates and Analytics data all arrive via provider change tickets, with lead time.

The Cato PS approach is a four-phase pipeline applied to every policy domain: EXPORT → REVIEW & MAP → DEPLOY (monitor-first) → OPTIMISE. The cardinal rule is never lift-and-shift: a rulebase that grew organically across template revisions is full of dead rules, duplicate objects and any-any debt. Rationalise on Versa Analytics hit counts first, translate only the rules that still earn their place, and let CMA events prove each rule before it blocks anything.

This page covers the policy workstream only. The network side — eBGP hub co-existence, branch cutover waves, per-spoke rollback and the carrier-contract sequencing — is the companion page, Versa Networks (SD-WAN & SASE) to Cato.

Provenance — read before you present

This page is authored from vendor documentation and Cato PS methodology research; it is not a delivery-verified runbook. Review the mappings, the TLS-inspection staging and anything contract-dependent with Cato Professional Services before committing it to a customer plan.

Domain 1 — firewall rules

VOS security policy & attached UTM

The vendor construct. VOS enforces zone-based access policies on the branch appliance: rules match zone pairs, address and service objects, applications and users, and each rule can carry attached UTM profiles — vulnerability (IPS), anti-virus and URL-filtering — so threat prevention is a per-rule property of the box. On shared appliances, per-tenant organisations partition the config; across the estate, the policy a branch actually runs is generated from Director's template stack plus that device's bind data.

The export. Per-appliance configuration exports from Director (Appliances → Export Configuration) or from the VOS CLI (show configuration | display set); the staging/post-staging and device templates export as .cfg. In carrier-managed estates, raise the export request in week one — it is a provider change ticket, not a console click, and everything downstream waits on it. Export the templates and the per-appliance effective configs: the templates give you the common intent, the effective configs tell you what actually varies per site.

Semantic gotchas

  • One rulebase becomes two policies with opposite defaults. A VOS zone-based rulebase mixes east-west and egress in one table. Classify every rule by destination first: WAN-bound rules land in an allowlist (default deny), internet-bound rules in a blocklist (default allow). A rule copied into the wrong policy either opens nothing or opens everything.
  • Per-rule UTM becomes global layers. In VOS, two rules can carry different IPS or AV profiles; in Cato, IPS and NGAM are single cloud policies with monitor/block modes and allowlists. Translate profile variants into scoped exceptions — never try to rebuild per-rule signature tuning.
  • The template stack hides divergence. Translating a device template alone misses what bind data changed per site. Regenerate the effective config per appliance and diff against the template baseline — the diffs are your genuine site exceptions; everything else is common intent, expressed once.
  • Rule order and shadowing. Both models are first-match. Shadowed VOS rules (never matched because an earlier rule wins) show zero hits in Analytics — drop them at the clean-up stage rather than importing dead weight.

Strategy. Export early (tickets first in managed estates) → rebuild effective per-site policy → rationalise on Analytics hit counts and retire dead rules → split by destination into WAN/Internet/LAN policies → push objects via API or Terraform → deploy with IPS and NGAM in monitor mode → promote to block on CMA event evidence.

Ask yourself

“Could you produce the effective firewall policy for any single branch today — template stack plus bind data — and say which of its rules actually matched traffic last quarter? If that answer involves a provider ticket, how long does it take?”

Domain 2 — SWG policy

URL-filtering profiles → Internet Firewall categories

The vendor construct. VOS web filtering lives in URL-filtering profiles: category-based actions, reputation-based filtering, and custom blocklist/allowlist URL patterns, with block or inform pages for the user. Profiles attach per access-policy rule, so different user populations get different web postures by carrying different profiles. The profiles export inside the same template/appliance config export as the firewall rules — there is no separate SWG export step.

VOS URL-filtering elementCato constructMigration note
Category action: blockInternet Firewall block rule on a Cato URL categoryStart from Cato's default category baseline (the Internet Firewall wizard helps); add only deliberate deltas.
Category action: alert / informAllow rule with event trackingCato separates action from tracking — alert-only intent becomes allow + track, visible in Events.
Custom blocklist URL patternsCustom categories (blocked)Carry only lists that encode real policy; retire entries that duplicate a Cato category.
Allowlist / override patternsNarrow allow rules ordered above the blockScope by user group and app, not bare wildcards — overrides are where debt accumulates.
Reputation-based filteringCato security categories + IPS / Anti-Malware layersMalicious-site blocking is a platform layer, not a profile — do not re-model it as URL rules.
Per-rule profile variants (per population)Identity-scoped Internet Firewall rulesEach profile variant becomes a block of rules scoped to a SCIM-provisioned group.
Block / inform pagesCato block page and prompt behaviourCommunicate the change: users see a new page, and helpdesk should expect the first-week questions.

Semantic gotchas

  • Category taxonomies never line up 1:1. Map by intent, not by name: build a worksheet of each blocked Versa category → the Cato category (or categories) that covers the same intent, and get the business owner to sign it — this is a policy decision, not a technical one.
  • Profile-per-rule becomes order-per-identity. Where VOS attached stricter profiles to specific rules, Cato expresses the same thing as identity-scoped rules in order. The SCIM groups must exist before those rules can be written.
  • Fidelity depends on TLS inspection. Without TLS inspection Cato classifies on domain/SNI; full-path URL matching and precise app identification need TLSi. Stage it (by category, then pilot groups) before judging category-match parity against the Versa estate.
  • Retire the compensations. Long custom URL lists often exist to patch gaps in the old engine's categorisation. Test each list against Cato's categories in monitor mode before carrying it over.

Strategy. Adopt the Cato default category baseline, layer the signed-off deltas from the worksheet, recreate population variants as group-scoped rules, and run the whole policy with tracking before enforcing — a week of events answers every “will this break the business” question with data.

Domain 3 — CASB policy · greenfield

CASB: not a translation — an upgrade

There is usually nothing to export. Typical Versa estates were bought as SD-WAN with on-box UTM; an inline CASB was rarely deployed, and SaaS control — where it exists at all — is a few URL-filtering categories doing a job they were never designed for. Frame this section honestly in the room: this is not a migration gap, it is the upgrade the customer is buying. The CASB policy is built greenfield from Cato best practice, not translated from a rulebase that does not exist.

What the estate does contribute: Versa Analytics application reports seed the sanctioned-app conversation — which SaaS the business already lives in — and the URL-filtering worksheet from the SWG domain flags any informal SaaS blocks worth preserving as proper CASB rules.

In the Versa estateCato constructWhat changes
Analytics application reportsApp Analytics + Cloud Apps DashboardContinuous shadow-IT discovery with risk scores, not periodic reports from a cluster you host.
URL categories doubling as SaaS controlCASB application rules with granular activity controlsAllow the app but govern the activity — upload, download, share — instead of a binary domain block.
Nothing — no app risk modelApplication catalogue with risk scoringSanctioned / tolerated / unsanctioned decisions made on evidence, per app.
Nothing — no tenant awarenessTenant restrictions on sanctioned appsCorporate tenant allowed, personal instances of the same app controlled.

Build sequence (best practice). Stage TLS inspection first — CASB activity-level control depends on it. Run shadow-IT discovery over a representative business cycle in App Analytics and the Cloud Apps Dashboard; classify the estate into sanctioned / tolerated / unsanctioned with the risk scores as evidence; write CASB rules in monitor mode; review the hits with the business owners; then enforce, strictest-risk apps first.

Position it as the upgrade it is

“Day one on Cato you will see every SaaS app in use, scored for risk — visibility the current estate has never had. We turn that into control at your pace: monitor first, then enforce.” That is a selling point, not a migration risk.

Domain 4 — DLP policy · greenfield

DLP: build from the data, not from a rulebase

Again, nothing to export. VOS estates almost never carry data-loss prevention; the nearest construct is file-type handling inside anti-virus profiles, which is malware hygiene, not data protection. Like CASB, this is a greenfield build — and the input is not a config file but a conversation: a short data-protection workshop with the compliance or security owner on what data matters (cardholder data, PII, credentials, source code), where it lives, and who legitimately moves it.

In the Versa estateCato constructWhat changes
File-type blocking in AV profilesDLP file-type controls inside content-aware rulesFile type becomes one condition among many, not the whole policy.
Nothing — no content awarenessDLP content profiles with predefined data types (PCI, PII, credentials, source code)Start from the predefined types — do not hand-build patterns on day one.
Nothing — no SaaS-aware data policyDLP rules scoped by app, category, user and direction“Finance may upload PCI to the sanctioned tenant; nobody uploads it anywhere else.”
Nothing — no evidence trailData Protection Dashboard + DLP eventsEvery match attributed to user, app and data type — the audit trail the estate never had.

Build sequence (best practice). TLS inspection staged first — DLP cannot inspect what it cannot see. Enable the predefined data types that match the workshop outcomes in monitor mode; review matches on the Data Protection Dashboard and tune false positives with the data owners; enforce on the highest-confidence rules first (cardholder data before “possible PII”), and widen coverage iteratively. Day-one blocking is how DLP projects die — monitor-mode evidence is how they succeed.

IPS and NGAM run as single global policies (monitor first), and the URLF-Standard category deltas become Internet Firewall category rules per the SWG domain — none of it reappears per rule.

What changed in translation

  • Per-rule UTM became global layers. URLF-Standard, IPS-Branch and AV-Default vanish from the rule table: IPS and NGAM are single cloud policies deployed in monitor mode and promoted on event evidence, and web-category intent moves into Internet Firewall category rules. Profile variants — Guest-Strict versus Standard — survive as scoped rules, not signature tuning.
  • Template and bind-data indirection became explicit scopes. {$v_LAN1_Subnet} only meant something after Director resolved it per device; in CMA the same intent is a named site group (UK-Branches) readable on the rule itself. Regenerating the effective config per appliance — the template-stack gotcha above — is what proved which sites the variable actually covered.
  • Zones and segments became scopes. The Zone-LAN → Zone-Internet pair told you this slice belongs in the Internet Firewall (blocklist, default allow); the guest zone survives as a network-range scope on the block rule rather than a zone object. WAN-bound zone pairs would have landed in the WAN Firewall instead.
  • Hit counts did the rationalisation. Four VOS rules became three CMA rules: Legacy-FTP-Allow showed zero hits in Versa Analytics and was retired, not translated — dead weight discarded at the clean-up stage, exactly as the pipeline demands.
Cross-domain best practices

The rules that hold across all four domains

🧭

Work with the default postures

Cato's WAN Firewall is an allowlist (default deny); the Internet Firewall is a blocklist (default allow). Classify every legacy rule by destination first and let each policy's default do the heavy lifting — do not recreate any-any scaffolding.

🔍

Stage TLS inspection early

TLSi is the fidelity prerequisite for the SWG, CASB and DLP domains alike — URL paths, app activities and content are invisible without it. Phase it by URL category and pilot group (the PS reference is four phases over four weeks) before judging policy parity.

⏱️

Monitor, then block

Every domain deploys watching first: firewall rules tracked, IPS and NGAM in monitor, CASB and DLP in monitor mode. Promotion to block is an evidence decision made from events, never an assumption.

🪪

Identity is a dependency, not a detail

Group-scoped rules across all four domains depend on IdP integration and SCIM-provisioned groups. Stand identity up in the foundation phase — before mapping begins — or the mapped policy has nothing to bind to.

📊

Validate with events and hit counts

Rationalise the old estate on Versa Analytics hit data; validate the new one on CMA events and rule hits. The same discipline retires dead rules on the way out and proves live rules on the way in.

✂️

Never lift-and-shift

Every rule imported unexamined is debt transferred at par. The clean-up stage — dead rules out, duplicates collapsed, any-any debt flagged — is the highest-value hour of the whole policy workstream.

The policy pipeline

Four domains, one pipeline

Every domain rides the same pipeline. The two rulebase domains (firewall, SWG) enter with an export; the two greenfield domains (CASB, DLP) enter with discovery inputs — Analytics app data and the data-protection workshop — and skip straight past translation to a best-practice build. Nothing reaches enforcement without surviving the clean-up filter and a monitor-mode soak.

Deploy — monitor mode first Enforce — promoted on evidence Discarded — dead rules never migrate
The four policy domains ride one pipeline: export (a provider change ticket in carrier-managed estates), clean-up on hit counts, mapping to CMA constructs, deployment in monitor mode, and promotion to enforcement on event evidence. The greenfield domains carry discovery inputs rather than rulebases — they skip translation, not the monitor-first discipline.
