Resilient Site Design

Every site fails eventually — a digger through the fibre, a dead power supply, a PoP taken out of rotation. Resilience isn't a default you inherit; it's a design decision made per site class, and a kiosk shouldn't get the same design (or the same spend) as a datacentre. This page sets out the reference patterns from small branch to critical DC, the Cato building blocks behind them — dual last miles, LTE last resort, HA Socket pairs, PoP failover and off-cloud recovery — and the runbook to prove they actually work.

Business objective

Design against the failure, not the brochure

Most availability conversations start with an SLA percentage and end with an invoice. A better conversation starts with the question: what exactly can fail at this site, and what happens to the people in the building when it does? On Cato there are four distinct failure domains, and each has its own building block — so you can match resilience (and cost) to how much each site actually matters.

1 · Last-mile loss

The everyday failure: a cut circuit, a provider maintenance window, a degraded DSL line. Answered by a second last mile with SLA-driven steering — and LTE/5G as the final backstop.

2 · Socket hardware loss

A failed PSU or a bricked device. Answered by an HA Socket pair on sites that can't wait for a hardware swap — and a cold-spare process everywhere else.

3 · PoP unreachable or degraded

Rarer: trouble between the ISP and the Cato PoP, or a PoP out of rotation. Answered automatically — the Socket evaluates its links and moves to another PoP, with preferred primary and secondary locations if you want to steer it.

4 · Total off-cloud

The site is powered, the LAN is up, but no PoP is reachable. Answered by recovery modes: internet traffic breaks out locally, WAN traffic re-forms over Socket-to-Socket DTLS tunnels, and the local LAN firewall keeps east-west policy running.

Per-second SLA steering

The Socket scores every link every second on loss, jitter and latency, and distributes flows by bandwidth ratio. A degrading link sheds traffic before users open tickets.

👥

HA without drama

Standby takes over after 3 s of missed keepalives, announcing itself with GARP for fast Layer-2 convergence — and the Cato Cloud maintains flow state through the transition.

📶

LTE that doesn't surprise finance

Last-resort links carry only minimal Cato system traffic while idle, precisely to keep cellular data cost near zero until the day you need it.

🌐

Graceful degradation off-cloud

Internet Recovery, Socket-to-Socket WAN Recovery tunnels and the on-Socket LAN firewall mean “no PoP reachable” degrades the site — it doesn't kill it.

Dual last miles — active/active with link precedence

WAN links take a precedence role: 1 (active), 2 (passive) or 3 (last resort). Active/active distribution is flow-based (5-tuple), so a conversation sticks to one link; flows are shared in proportion to configured bandwidth — a 100 Mbps and a 20 Mbps link get flows at roughly 5:1. Every second, each link is scored against health thresholds (packet loss 3%, jitter 30 ms, latency 600 ms); links that miss them are deprioritised, and a dead link's flows move to the surviving link immediately. The Socket steers upstream; the PoP steers downstream — so both directions are managed. Passive links activate when all active links are down or fail the Connection SLA, and stand down after 10 minutes of restored health on the higher-precedence links.

LTE / 5G as last resort — and its caveats

A last-resort (precedence 3) link — typically cellular, via the X1600 LTE Socket or an external modem — activates only when all active and passive links are down or fail the SLA thresholds, and deactivates once a precedence 1 or 2 link has met the SLA for a continuous 10 minutes. Design caveats from the documentation worth repeating in the room:

  • While idle it carries only minimal Cato system traffic — good for cost, but it means the cellular path is not continuously proven under load. Test it.
  • Off-cloud (Socket-to-Socket) traffic is not supported over last-resort links, and off-cloud transport is automatically disabled on precedence-3 links.
  • An optional grace timer (default 0 s) delays activation to ride out blips — but any value above 0 means the site is dark during the wait.
  • Recommended configuration: exclude the link from last-mile monitoring and relax its keepalive settings, so monitoring itself doesn't burn the data plan.

HA Socket pairs — requirements and readiness

Socket HA is an active/standby pair of the same model family (X1500 pairs with X1500B; an X1600 cannot pair with an X1600 LTE), supported for physical sites and AWS/Azure vSockets. Cabling must be symmetrical — both Sockets mirrored into the same LAN and the same ISPs — with the secondary's management IP inside the site's native range. The pair exchanges VRRP keepalives over a designated LAN port (default VRID 100). Failover triggers: 3 seconds of missed keepalives, primary hardware/power failure, or the master losing all internet connectivity for more than 10 seconds; the standby announces itself with GARP, and an HA event is raised if the secondary stays active beyond 35 seconds. The CMA rolls health into one word — Ready — built from three indicators:

IndicatorGreen means
ConnectedBoth Sockets hold their own working tunnel to the Cato Cloud
KeepaliveExactly one primary and one secondary are talking — this is also the split-brain check
Compatible VersionBoth Sockets run the same major Socket OS version
ReadyAll three OK — the site can fail over cleanly. Anything else is a ticket, not trivia.

PoP selection and failover

By default a connectivity algorithm picks the best PoP — the recommendation is to leave it on Automatic and set the site's city. For critical sites you can define preferred primary and secondary PoP locations, and optionally restrict the site to them. If every link fails the SLA against the current PoP, the Socket connects to a different PoP and runs an initial SLA evaluation of 40–50 seconds — returning to the original if nothing better is found. A Socket that lands on a non-preferred PoP automatically moves back after 60 minutes by default (tunable or disabled).

Off-cloud: what still works when no PoP is reachable

The Socket is not a thin client — it degrades deliberately when the Cato Cloud is unreachable:

  • Internet Recovery (enabled by default) — on tunnel loss (100% packet loss, or three consecutive missed keepalives) the Socket designates a link and sends internet traffic straight out via the local ISP from its own public IP. Reconnection uses a back-off (8 s doubling up to 1024 s) to prevent flapping, and traffic returns to Cato the moment keepalives resume.
  • WAN Recovery — site-to-site WAN traffic re-forms over encrypted off-cloud DTLS tunnels between Sockets, full mesh by default. Estates with hundreds of sites can use a hub-and-spoke off-cloud topology instead — spokes tunnel to hub sites only, which trades spoke-to-spoke recovery for far less tunnel/keepalive load on small Sockets. Off-cloud traffic doesn't count against the site's bandwidth licence; sites need unique public IPs, and a Socket supports at most three off-cloud links.
  • Alt-WAN Recovery (limited release) — sites keeping an MPLS/alt-WAN underlay can automatically reroute via it to another Socket site, which forwards traffic on to the Cato Cloud. Not compatible with BGP-connected sites.
  • The LAN keeps its firewall — the Socket Next Gen LAN Firewall enforces Layer 2–7 east-west policy on the Socket itself and routes matching traffic locally, so segmentation, OT and IoT keep operating even with the internet down entirely.
  • IPsec and cloud-interconnect sites get none of this — no Internet or WAN Recovery. Their resilience is tunnel design: primary and secondary tunnels to two different PoPs.
Recovery is a degraded mode — treat it like one

Traffic in Internet, WAN or Alt-WAN Recovery bypasses Cato's cloud inspection — firewall, IPS and threat protection are not applied, and off-cloud transport is likewise uninspected. Recommended practice: decide per site class whether that trade is acceptable (a PCI estate may prefer to disable Internet Recovery and accept the outage), and alert on recovery events so nobody quietly runs uninspected for a week.

BGP for the sites that route

Datacentres and MPLS-coexistence sites should speak BGP to the Cato Cloud over the LAN or alt-WAN interface — no tunnels required — so routes are learned dynamically instead of maintained by hand, inheriting the site's security policy. The resilience property that matters: when a Socket disconnects, its site's ranges are withdrawn, so traffic immediately follows the next-best BGP path — the standard active/standby DC pattern. BFD can be configured for BGP neighbours to speed failure detection. Two limits to design around: no graceful restart for route withdrawal, and 1024 received prefixes per neighbour by default.
