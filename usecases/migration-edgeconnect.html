HPE Aruba EdgeConnect (Silver Peak) to Cato

A typical EdgeConnect estate is SD-WAN-only by design: EC appliances and Business Intent Overlays handle the WAN, while full security inspection is service-chained to a separate SSE vendor — two consoles, two policy models, two contracts. This page maps each EdgeConnect construct to its Cato equivalent and walks a five-phase path: early Orchestrator export, eBGP hub co-existence with the EC fabric left intact, regional cutover waves with mirror-image rollback, security consolidation wave by wave — and an honest treatment of what Boost does that Cato deliberately does differently.

Business objective

Three decision points: an SD-WAN-only estate, a separate SSE vendor, a portfolio in flux

EdgeConnect customers run the former Silver Peak platform: EC appliances (or EC-V virtual instances) at branches and data centres, driven from Orchestrator as SaaS or self-hosted. Its signature constructs are up to seven Business Intent Overlays — each with its own topology, link-bonding policy, QoS priority and internet-breakout behaviour — tunnel bonding with path conditioning (forward error correction, packet order correction) for private-line-like behaviour over broadband, First-Packet iQ application steering, and Boost, the WAN-optimisation pack licensed separately per-Mbps. The security design centre, though, is service chaining to a cloud SSE partner: Orchestrator has native workflows that build IPsec/GRE tunnels to Zscaler, Netskope and others, and HPE's own SSE — the former Axis Security platform, acquired March 2023 — is a separate product with its own console and licensing, orchestrated the same way. A typical account is therefore running at least two policy planes.

The second decision point is the portfolio. HPE completed the $925M Silver Peak acquisition on 21 September 2020, then closed the ~$13.4bn Juniper Networks acquisition on 2 July 2025 — and now sells three branch-WAN propositions with three management planes: EdgeConnect SD-WAN (Orchestrator), EdgeConnect SD-Branch/Microbranch (Aruba Central) and Juniper Session Smart SD-WAN (Mist). No EdgeConnect end-of-life has been announced — say so honestly. But analysts flagged the rationalisation question well before close: Dell'Oro noted the HPE/Juniper overlap "is limited to SASE" and urged HPE to align quickly on one SSE; SDxCentral openly asked whether HPE would eliminate Juniper's SASE in favour of Aruba plus Axis. Asking HPE in writing which line carries the roadmap is legitimate customer diligence, not FUD. Meanwhile the lifecycle clocks keep running: HPE's published policy sets ECOS 9.4 End of Maintenance at 31 December 2026 and End of Support at 31 December 2028, so fleet upgrade planning continues for the life of the estate.

The objective of this migration: a thin Socket at the edge, elastic inspection and optimisation in the PoP, and one console — the Cato Management Application — for networking and security. No per-site bandwidth-tier or Boost sizing, no SSE tunnels to orchestrate, and the SD-WAN + SSE consolidation happens in a single move.

Two acquisitions, three lines

Silver Peak joined HPE in September 2020; Juniper closed in July 2025. Community buyer's guides describe the standard pattern — dual lines coexist 12–24 months before one goes to sustaining mode — and the Axis SSE vs Juniper Secure Edge overlap is unresolved. A written roadmap commitment is a reasonable procurement ask.

The SSE seam

Full inspection means Orchestrator-built tunnels to Zscaler, Netskope or HPE SSE: two consoles, two policy models, two support contracts, and tunnel orchestration as the operational seam. Every site cutover and every policy change touches both planes.

The appliance anchor

Sizing, HA pairs, per-appliance bandwidth-tier licences and movable Boost blocks all live at the branch, with hardware refresh and ECOS/Orchestrator compatibility to manage. Cato's counter is architectural: a thin Socket at the edge, elastic inspection and optimisation in the PoP.

Ask yourself

“When your EdgeConnect renewal lands, can HPE tell you in writing which of its three SD-WAN lines — and which SSE — carries the long-term roadmap? And do you know what Boost and your SSE subscription cost per year, side by side, across the estate?”

The honest Boost answer

One row above needs saying in plain words: Boost's byte-level data reduction and CIFS acceleration have no like-for-like Cato equivalent. Boost deduplicates between appliance pairs that both hold Boost capacity; Cato's optimisation is architectural. Never hand-wave this — quantify it in the PoV with the customer's real flows before any Boost-heavy site migrates.

What transfers

Latency-bound applications regularly get their fix from the architecture: split-TCP proxying at the PoP (larger effective windows, round-trip mitigation), packet-loss mitigation on the last mile, and a private backbone with optimised routing in the middle mile. First-mile loss and long-haul latency — the problems path conditioning and TCP acceleration were bought for — are handled platform-wide, with nothing metered per-Mbps.

What doesn't — and the honest mitigations

For bandwidth-bound bulk transfer over thin links the dedup benefit disappears. Mitigations, in order: right-size the last mile (broadband economics usually allow it); schedule replication windows; use application-native dedup/compression — modern backup and storage replication tools ship their own; or re-architect the workload (cloud storage/DR). CIFS over distance is an application-architecture item — DFS placement, cloud file services — not a network-feature negotiation.

The migration path

Five phases from Orchestrator to one platform

Provenance — read before you present

This page is authored from vendor documentation and Cato PS methodology research; it is not a delivery-verified runbook. Review the design — especially the eBGP handoff, subnet-sharing propagation, filtering and rollback details, and anything contract-dependent — with Cato Professional Services before committing it to a customer plan.

The arc follows the Cato PS four-phase methodology (Discover & Design → Pilot, Build & Initial Rollout → Advanced Security & Broader Rollout → Tune, Optimise & Handover; the reference plan is four months), with co-existence and security consolidation broken out because they are the load-bearing steps in an EdgeConnect estate.

  1. Phase 1 — Discover & design: export Orchestrator early, inventory intent

    Run Orchestrator's scheduled backup (Orchestrator database plus per-appliance backups), export the template groups, and pull any preconfiguration YAML — sites built via preconfig are self-documenting; sites configured by hand are not, so diff templates against per-appliance state. The pyedgeconnect SDK and Orchestrator REST API can bulk-export appliance and overlay configuration for offline analysis. Then build the overlay intent inventory — the core artefact: for each BIO record the match criteria, topology (mesh vs hub-and-spoke, regions), link-bonding policy, QoS priority, brownout thresholds and internet-breakout behaviour (direct, backhaul, or service-chained to which SSE). That table is the customer's network policy — collapse duplicate intents rather than transliterating seven overlays.

    In parallel: the Boost-dependency inventory — Orchestrator's data-reduction reports show per-application reduction ratios; separate workloads that materially depend on deduplication (replication, backup, CIFS over distance) from those that mainly benefit from latency mitigation. Audit routing (LAN-side OSPF/BGP peer types — Branch/Branch-transit routes are shared into the fabric, BGP-PE routes deliberately are not — plus subnet-sharing metrics, statics, inbound NAT). Inventory the SSE estate: which BIOs chain to which tenant, what policy lives in the SSE console, contract end dates and notice periods, remote-access population. Classify sites (hubs, HA pairs, EC-V, any Aruba Central SD-Branch population — a different product, scoped explicitly in or out), flag dual-broadband sites surviving on heavy FEC for circuit upgrade, fix the co-existence design (eBGP handoff, AS numbering — the Cato side is eBGP-only, default ASN 64515 — filtering, cutover template per site class), and stand up the foundation: CMA baseline, IdP/SCIM, Client packaging, TLS-inspection certificates, socket logistics, SIEM planning.

  2. Phase 2 — Co-existence: parallel Sockets at the EC hubs (PS Option F)

    Network → Sites

    Stand up Sockets (or vSockets) at the data centres and at each EdgeConnect hub site, alongside the EC appliances; establish eBGP handoffs into each hub's LAN-side routing and validate route exchange, symmetry and failover before any branch moves. The EdgeConnect fabric — subnet sharing and all BIOs — stays fully intact on its side of the handoff. Confirm in design that Cato-learned prefixes actually propagate into subnet sharing at the hub (peer typing matters: BGP-PE-learned routes are not shared). Filter the default route and the prefixes of any half-migrated site at every handoff; use BGP summaries and Cato's BGP filtering to keep the exchange tidy. Convert policy from the BIO inventory: network rules and bandwidth profiles recreating overlay steering intent (real-time, business-critical, bulk), WAN Firewall for any BIO topology restrictions, the Internet Firewall wizard absorbing the breakout policy, IPS/NGAM in monitor-then-block.

  3. Phase 3 — Regional branch waves: cutover as a routing event

    Monitor → Topology

    Cut over one representative branch first and soak it, then migrate region by region behind each regional hub handoff: simple single-WAN branches → HA-pair branches → complex sites (local inbound services, Boost-heavy sites, EC-V hosts) → hubs last. Each spoke is a routing event: 1) disable the EdgeConnect spoke in Orchestrator so its subnets are withdrawn from subnet sharing and the eBGP exchange; 2) bring up the Cato socket site; 3) routes propagate automatically to both domains; 4) rollback is the mirror image — down the socket site, re-enable the EC spoke, and routing reconverges. No per-site route engineering during the window.

  4. Phase 4 — Security consolidation, wave by wave

    Security → TLS Inspection

    Security moves with each site's cutover: the socket sends all traffic to the Cato PoP, so the site's SSE tunnels are simply not rebuilt — retire the Orchestrator SSE tunnel config for migrated sites rather than leaving dead tunnels, and keep the SSE tenant alive until its last consuming site and user cohort have moved. Migrate SSE/VPN user populations in parallel cohorts to the Cato Client, re-modelling ZTNA policy with Device Posture. Enable TLS inspection in phases by category and test group (PS reference: four phases over four weeks), then CASB discovery, tenant controls and DLP monitor-mode → enforcement. Time SSE contract notice against the final wave, not the first.

  5. Phase 5 — Tune, re-baseline Boost, decommission

    Monitor → Events

    Tune QoS against the former BIO priority model and validate security controls; bring the SIEM integration live. For sites flagged Boost-dependent in Phase 1, measure the workload on Cato during the wave window and apply the mitigations — last-mile sizing, replication windows, application-native dedup — before the EC pair is switched off. Then decommission in order: remove eBGP handoffs as each region completes → retire EC hub appliances → cancel or let lapse bandwidth-tier, Boost and SSE subscriptions per the contract map → decommission self-hosted Orchestrator (or close the OaaS tenancy) once config and reporting exports are archived → return or dispose of appliances. Close with documentation, admin training and operational handover.

Co-existence & rollback

Two fabrics, one eBGP seam

Asymmetry is the enemy. With two overlays joined in more than one place, a flow can exit through one region's hub and return through another — breaking stateful inspection on the EdgeConnect firewalls. The PS multi-region pattern (Option F — demonstrated against a FortiGate estate and applied unchanged here, because the legacy hub only needs to speak eBGP, which EdgeConnect supports on the LAN side) puts a parallel Socket at each EdgeConnect hub with an eBGP handoff, and lets AS-path length anchor each region's traffic to its local hub: cross-region routes carry an extra inter-hub AS hop, so traffic exits and returns through the same hub. The EdgeConnect overlay — subnet sharing plus BIO tunnels — stays intact and segmented on its side of the handoff.

Where BGP is unavailable at a hub

The PS static alternative is an interconnect site with routed ranges — pre-stage real subnets on the interconnect and dummy ranges on the future Cato sites; at cutover delete the routed range and update the dummy range; rollback is re-adding the statics. Slower per site than BGP, but every step remains reversible.

Security plane during transition

Migrated sites are inspected by Cato; unmigrated sites keep their EC→SSE service chains untouched. Freeze both security policies except for mirrored changes during the window, and run one client (SSE/VPN or Cato) active per endpoint per cohort. Published/inbound services on EC appliances migrate deliberately — re-publish via Cato (remote port forwarding or static-IP egress) or leave them on a retained leg until their own change window.

Gotchas & objections

What bites, and what they'll say

“Boost gives us 60–90% data reduction on replication — can Cato match that?”

Answer it honestly: no like-for-like feature exists. Boost's data reduction is byte-level deduplication between appliance pairs; Cato's optimisation is architectural — split-TCP proxying at the PoP, packet-loss mitigation on the last mile, a private backbone in the middle. For latency-bound applications this regularly is the fix; for bandwidth-bound bulk transfer over thin links the dedup benefit disappears, and the mitigations are last-mile sizing, replication windows, application-native dedup, or re-architecting the workload. Quantify in the PoV with the customer's real flows before the Boost-heavy sites migrate.

CIFS and WAN-op-dependent legacy apps

Boost's CIFS read-ahead/write-behind masks chatty-protocol latency. If discovery finds users opening file shares across continents, treat it as an application-architecture item — DFS placement, cloud file services — rather than a network-feature negotiation, and validate the experience on Cato's TCP acceleration during the pilot. An estate that has quietly grown dependent on WAN optimisation needs those workloads found in Phase 1, not at cutover.

Seven overlays don't become seven rule blocks

Up to seven BIOs can each have a different topology, bonding policy and breakout rule; Cato is one global fabric. Steering intent becomes app-aware network rules and bandwidth profiles; reachability intent (which overlay meshed where) becomes WAN Firewall policy. Most estates use far fewer distinct intents than they have overlays — collapse duplicates during the inventory rather than transliterating. Also flag dual-broadband sites surviving on heavy FEC: Cato mitigates loss with packet duplication across active/active links, but a genuinely poor circuit belongs on the upgrade list in the wave plan.

EC-V virtual estates

Cloud EC-Vs (AWS/Azure/GCP transit hubs) map to vSockets, native IPsec to the PoP, or cloud interconnect — decide per VPC/VNet during discovery. Hypervisor EC-Vs at branches usually become physical Sockets; check anything unusual — an EC-V on a host doing double duty — early, and put the disposition of every virtual instance in the Phase 1 estate classification.

“First-Packet iQ steers on packet one — a PoP round trip must be slower”

Cato classifies and inspects in the PoP on the same single pass; the PoP is the security edge, so there is no appliance-then-SWG double hop as in the EC + SSE service-chain model. Compare end-to-end paths — branch→SSE PoP versus branch→Cato PoP — not packet-one behaviour in isolation.

“HPE says EdgeConnect is strategic — the Juniper deal changes nothing”

Possibly true; no end-of-life is announced, and say so. But the customer is entitled to ask which of three SD-WAN lines and which of two management clouds (Aruba Central vs Mist) carries the long-term roadmap, and what happens to the Axis SSE vs Juniper Secure Edge overlap. Analyst and community commentary consistently frames this as a rationalisation-in-waiting. A written roadmap commitment is a reasonable procurement ask — and the comparison is between that uncertainty and a single-vendor, single-platform alternative.

Scope and PoV hygiene

“EdgeConnect SD-Branch” (Aruba Central-managed gateways and Microbranch APs) is a different product with a different console — scope it explicitly in or out, and don't assume Orchestrator exports cover it. Routing edge cases — subnet-sharing metrics, BGP-PE non-sharing, overlay-specific route policies, inbound NAT on EC appliances — should be listed in discovery and validated in the PoV by name, not assumed.
