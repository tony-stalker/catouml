EdgeConnect Policy Migration

An EdgeConnect estate splits its policy across two planes: steering, QoS and breakout intent live in up to seven Business Intent Overlays in Orchestrator, while web security is usually service-chained to a separate SSE tenant with its own console and rulebase. This page works the translation domain by domain — overlay intent, zone firewall and QoS classes into CMA network rules, bandwidth profiles and firewall policy; SWG extracted from whichever SSE actually holds it; CASB and DLP as greenfield best-practice builds — through one pipeline: export, rationalise, map to CMA constructs, deploy in monitor mode, enforce on evidence.

Business objective

Two policy planes — and the deepest translation is intent

Policy is the long pole in almost every SD-WAN and security migration, and EdgeConnect adds a twist of its own: the policy is not in one place. Steering, QoS priority, topology and internet-breakout behaviour live in up to seven Business Intent Overlays in Orchestrator; appliance-level access control lives in the zone-based firewall; and full web inspection usually lives on another vendor's platform entirely — Orchestrator's design centre is service chaining to a cloud SSE (Zscaler, Netskope or HPE Aruba SSE), each with its own console, licensing and policy model. “Export the policy” is therefore two export projects across two vendors — and the deepest translation is not a rulebase at all, but intent: what each overlay was for.

The Cato PS approach is a four-phase pipeline applied to every policy domain: EXPORT → REVIEW & MAP → DEPLOY (monitor-first) → OPTIMISE. The cardinal rule is never lift-and-shift: seven overlays are usually three or four genuine intents wearing different topologies, and a zone rulebase grown across template revisions carries dead rules and duplicate objects. Collapse and rationalise first, translate only what still earns its place, and let CMA events prove each rule before it blocks — or shapes — anything.

This page covers the policy workstream only. The network side — eBGP hub co-existence with subnet sharing, branch cutover waves, Boost re-baselining and per-spoke rollback — is the companion page, HPE Aruba EdgeConnect (Silver Peak) to Cato.

Domain 1 — FW rules & QoS

Overlay intent, zone rules & QoS classes

The vendor construct. EdgeConnect ships a zone-based firewall on the appliance (with IDPS in the Advanced tier), but the constructs that actually encode policy are the Business Intent Overlays: up to seven application-specific WAN overlays, each defining its own match criteria (applications, DSCP, ACLs), topology (full mesh vs hub-and-spoke, per region), link-bonding policy, QoS priority, brownout thresholds and internet-breakout behaviour, with First-Packet iQ classifying flows on packet one to select the overlay. The signature work of this migration is re-expressing that overlay intent as Cato constructs — network rules for steering, bandwidth profiles for priority, WAN Firewall for reachability, Internet Firewall for breakout.

The export. Run Orchestrator's scheduled backup (Orchestrator database plus per-appliance backups), export the template groups, and pull any preconfiguration YAML — preconfig-built sites are self-documenting; hand-built sites are not, so diff the templates against per-appliance state to find silent divergence. The pyedgeconnect SDK and Orchestrator REST API bulk-export appliance and overlay configuration for offline analysis. The core artefact is the overlay-intent inventory: one row per BIO recording match criteria, topology, bonding policy, QoS priority, thresholds and breakout policy (direct, backhaul, or which SSE). That table is the customer's network policy.

Semantic gotchas

  • Seven overlays rarely mean seven intents. Most estates express three or four genuine intents across their BIOs. Collapse duplicates during the inventory — transliterating each overlay into its own rule block imports structure, not policy.
  • Topology is policy in disguise. A BIO restricted to hub-and-spoke was often doing segmentation. On Cato, reachability is explicit WAN Firewall policy — write the intent as rules rather than mourning the tunnels.
  • One rulebase becomes two policies with opposite defaults. Classify every zone rule by destination first: WAN-bound rules land in an allowlist (default deny), internet-bound rules in a blocklist (default allow). A rule copied into the wrong policy either opens nothing or opens everything.
  • First-Packet iQ is not a policy artefact. It steers on packet one; Cato classifies at the PoP on the same single pass as inspection, and there is no appliance-then-SWG double hop to preserve. Compare end-to-end paths, not packet-one behaviour in isolation.
  • Templates hide divergence. Translating a template alone misses what changed per site. Diff template groups against per-appliance effective config — the diffs are the genuine site exceptions; everything else is common intent, expressed once.

Strategy. Export early (Orchestrator backup + templates + preconfig YAML) → build the overlay-intent inventory → collapse duplicate intents → split zone rules by destination into WAN / Internet / LAN policies → recreate steering as network rules and bandwidth profiles → push objects via API or Terraform → deploy with IPS in monitor mode → promote to enforcement on CMA event evidence.

Ask yourself

“How many of your overlays could you justify today — which applications actually ride each one, and why does its topology or QoS differ from the next? If two overlays share the same answer, that’s one Cato intent, not two.”

Domain 2 — SWG policy

Web policy lives in the SSE tenant — extract it there

The vendor construct. EdgeConnect's design centre for full web inspection is service chaining: Orchestrator's automated workflows build IPsec/GRE tunnels to a cloud SSE — Zscaler, Netskope, or HPE's own SSE (the former Axis Security platform). The consequence for the policy workstream is that there is usually no EdgeConnect web policy to export: the SWG rulebase lives in the SSE tenant, and Orchestrator only knows which sites tunnel to it. What Orchestrator does contribute is the scoping map — which BIOs at which sites break out directly, and which chain to which SSE.

Semantic gotchas

  • One estate, several web postures. Different BIOs can break out differently — some direct, some backhauled, some chained — so different site classes may run different web policy today. Inventory who has what before assuming one policy exists.
  • Freeze both planes during the window. Orchestrator and the SSE tenant are two policy planes with a tunnel seam between them. Keep both frozen except for mirrored changes, and keep the SSE tenant alive until its last consuming site and user cohort have moved.
  • Don't rebuild the chain. The temptation is to service-chain Cato to the incumbent SSE “temporarily”. Resist it: migrated sites get single-pass inspection at the PoP; unmigrated sites keep their EC → SSE chains untouched until their wave.
  • Category parity needs TLS inspection. Without TLSi, Cato classifies on domain/SNI; full-path URL matching and precise app identification need it. Stage TLSi before judging match parity against the SSE tenant.

Strategy. Map which sites chain where (from the Orchestrator tunnel config) → run the relevant SSE policy page's export-and-translate workstream for that tenant → greenfield the direct-breakout population from the wizard baseline → run the whole policy with tracking before enforcing — a week of events answers every “will this break the business” question with data.

Domain 3 — CASB policy · greenfield

CASB: not a translation — an upgrade

There is usually nothing to export. CASB never lived on EdgeConnect: the appliance steers and firewalls, it does not govern SaaS activity. Where the chained SSE tenant carried CASB licences, its inline rules translate via the relevant policy page — but in most EdgeConnect estates the honest answer is “nothing deployed”. Frame this section honestly in the room: this is not a migration gap, it is the upgrade the customer is buying. The CASB policy is built greenfield from Cato best practice, not translated from a rulebase that does not exist.

What the estate does contribute: First-Packet iQ application statistics in Orchestrator's dashboards seed the sanctioned-app conversation — which SaaS the business already lives in — and the SSE scoping map from the SWG domain flags any informal SaaS blocks worth preserving as proper CASB rules.

Build sequence (best practice). Stage TLS inspection first — CASB activity-level control depends on it. Run shadow-IT discovery over a representative business cycle in App Analytics and the Cloud Apps Dashboard; classify the estate into sanctioned / tolerated / unsanctioned with the risk scores as evidence; write CASB rules in monitor mode; review the hits with the business owners; then enforce, strictest-risk apps first.

Position it as the upgrade it is

“Day one on Cato you will see every SaaS app in use, scored for risk — visibility the WAN estate has never had, in the same console as the network. We turn that into control at your pace: monitor first, then enforce.” That is a selling point, not a migration risk.

Domain 4 — DLP policy · greenfield

DLP: build from the data, not from a rulebase

Again, nothing to export. EdgeConnect has no content awareness at all — the zone firewall and IDPS inspect traffic, not data. If the chained SSE tenant ran DLP, those dictionaries and profiles translate via its policy page (exact-match content is rebuilt from source, never exported). Otherwise this is a greenfield build, and the input is not a config file but a conversation: a short data-protection workshop with the compliance or security owner on what data matters (cardholder data, PII, credentials, source code), where it lives, and who legitimately moves it.

In the EdgeConnect estateCato constructWhat changes
Nothing on the appliance — no content awarenessDLP content profiles with predefined data types (PCI, PII, credentials, source code)Start from the predefined types — do not hand-build patterns on day one.
SSE-tenant DLP, where licensedDLP rules translated via the SSE policy pageDictionaries map by intent; exact-match datasets are re-indexed from the source data.
Nothing — no SaaS-aware data policyDLP rules scoped by app, category, user and direction“Finance may upload PCI to the sanctioned tenant; nobody uploads it anywhere else.”
Nothing — no evidence trailData Protection Dashboard + DLP eventsEvery match attributed to user, app and data type — the audit trail the estate never had.

Build sequence (best practice). TLS inspection staged first — DLP cannot inspect what it cannot see. Enable the predefined data types that match the workshop outcomes in monitor mode; review matches on the Data Protection Dashboard and tune false positives with the data owners; enforce on the highest-confidence rules first (cardholder data before “possible PII”), and widen coverage iteratively. Day-one blocking is how DLP projects die — monitor-mode evidence is how they succeed.

What changed in translation

  • One overlay became a rule and a profile, not a tunnel set. Each BIO’s steering and priority intent lands in two clean constructs — an application-aware network rule plus a bandwidth-management profile — instead of an overlay carrying topology, bonding and breakout in one object.
  • Topology settings dissolved into backbone routing. Dual hub-and-spoke versus full mesh is no longer configuration — every site rides the same global backbone, and there are no per-overlay topologies to manage. What the topology was doing (reachability) is stated explicitly as WAN Firewall rules.
  • The service-chained SWG became native single-pass inspection. The Default overlay’s tunnel to the SSE tenant has no successor: default traffic is inspected at the PoP on the same pass that classifies it, and the two-console seam disappears.
  • Boost-dependent flows were re-baselined, not translated. Where an overlay leaned on Boost (WAN optimisation, deduplication) there is no one-for-one equivalent — throughput expectations are re-baselined on evidence during the monitor soak, as covered on the network companion page.
Cross-domain best practices

The rules that hold across all four domains

🧭

Work with the default postures

Cato's WAN Firewall is an allowlist (default deny); the Internet Firewall is a blocklist (default allow). Classify every legacy rule by destination first and let each policy's default do the heavy lifting — do not recreate any-any scaffolding.

🔍

Stage TLS inspection early

TLSi is the fidelity prerequisite for the SWG, CASB and DLP domains alike — URL paths, app activities and content are invisible without it. Phase it by URL category and pilot group (the PS reference is four phases over four weeks) before judging policy parity.

⏱️

Monitor, then block

Every domain deploys watching first: firewall rules tracked, IPS in monitor, CASB and DLP in monitor mode. Promotion to block is an evidence decision made from events, never an assumption.

🪪

Identity is a dependency, not a detail

Group-scoped rules across all four domains depend on IdP integration and SCIM-provisioned groups. Stand identity up in the foundation phase — before mapping begins — or the mapped policy has nothing to bind to.

🧵

One rulebase replaces two planes

Orchestrator plus an SSE tenant is two consoles with a tunnel seam between them. During the window, freeze both except for mirrored changes; retire SSE tunnel config as each site migrates, and time contract notice against the final wave, not the first.

✂️

Never lift-and-shift

Seven overlays collapse into the three or four intents they express; dead zone rules and template-drift duplicates never migrate. The clean-up stage is the highest-value hour of the whole policy workstream.

The policy pipeline

Four domains, one pipeline

Every domain rides the same pipeline. The firewall/QoS domain enters with the Orchestrator export; the SWG domain enters with the incumbent SSE tenant's export; the two greenfield domains (CASB, DLP) enter with discovery inputs — First-Packet iQ app statistics and the data-protection workshop — and skip straight past translation to a best-practice build. Nothing reaches enforcement without surviving the clean-up filter and a monitor-mode soak.

Deploy — monitor mode first Enforce — promoted on evidence Discarded — dead rules and duplicate overlays never migrate
The four policy domains ride one pipeline: export (Orchestrator backup and templates on one side, the SSE tenant's console on the other), clean-up where seven overlays collapse into their genuine intents and dead rules are cut, mapping to CMA constructs, deployment in monitor mode, and promotion to enforcement on event evidence. The greenfield domains carry discovery inputs rather than rulebases — they skip translation, not the monitor-first discipline.
