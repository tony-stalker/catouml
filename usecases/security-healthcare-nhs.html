UK Healthcare: NHS DSPT & CAF-Aligned SASE

Every NHS organisation — and every supplier that touches patient data — answers to the Data Security and Protection Toolkit, and since September 2024 the large-organisation track is an NCSC CAF-aligned, independently audited assessment. The evidence it asks for is network-shaped: segmented clinical systems, isolated medical devices, MFA on every remote path, monitored supplier access. This use case maps Cato to those asks — with LAN Firewall segmentation at the Socket, Device Inventory for the IoMT estate, and one evidence plane for the submission.

Business objective

Why this matters

The Data Security and Protection Toolkit (DSPT) is the annual assurance submission for health and care organisations in England. In September 2024 it was rebuilt for large organisations around the NCSC Cyber Assessment Framework — 47 contributing outcomes with achievement levels, replacing the old assertion checklist. The current published edition is DSPT 2025-26 ("version 8"), aligned to CAF version 3.4, whose final submission deadline of 30 June 2026 has just passed — so most organisations are now between cycles, digesting audit findings and improvement plans.

For the generic Cyber Essentials and CAF story — the five CE control themes, the CE-to-Cato mapping, CAF 4.0's objectives A–D and the honest-positioning rules — see the companion page UK Public Sector: Cyber Essentials & NCSC CAF. This page goes deep on what is healthcare-specific: the DSPT's tracks and deadlines, clinical network segmentation, IoMT and legacy device isolation, resilient community connectivity, and supplier access after a supplier compromise became a patient-safety incident.

Two tracks — know which conversation you are in

CAF-aligned track

NHS trusts, Integrated Care Boards, CSUs, DHSC arm's-length bodies, NIS-designated operators of essential services, genomics organisations and Key IT Suppliers. Outcome-based against CAF objectives A–E, with mandatory independent audit (2025-26 scope published October 2025) and improvement-plan updates due 31 December. This is an evidence-of-outcomes conversation.

Standards-based track

GP practices, care homes, pharmacies, dental practices, opticians and most smaller suppliers still complete the "classic" DSPT against the ten National Data Guardian standards — assertions plus evidence, with GP-specific items such as clinical system access controls. A GP-federation or care-group conversation is still an assertion-and-evidence conversation.

Staleness check — verify before you present

As of mid-July 2026 no 2026-27 DSPT edition ("v9") has been published — expect it on the established ~September cadence, and check the DSPT news page before quoting deadlines. Note also the version lag: the NHS regime is aligned to CAF v3.4 while NCSC's current published CAF is v4.0 (August 2025) — the NHS has not yet adopted v4.0.

The patient-safety stakes

When NHS networks fail, patients feel it

Two public incidents anchor every UK healthcare security conversation — one that made segmentation and patching a board issue, and one that made supplier access a patient-safety issue.

WannaCry May 2017

At least 81 of 236 trusts affected; 6,912 confirmed cancelled appointments (NHS England estimated over 19,000); five A&E departments diverted patients. The NAO's root causes: unpatched or unsupported Windows and poor internet-facing firewall management — "a relatively unsophisticated attack" that basic practice would have prevented.

London pathology ransomware June 2024

Qilin ransomware encrypted Synnovis, the pathology provider for two south-east London trusts. 10,152 outpatient appointments and 1,710 elective procedures postponed; blood testing fell to ~10% of normal, triggering a national O-type blood appeal. A supplier compromise with no lateral containment became a clinical incident.

The first linked death June 2025

A patient-safety investigation at King's College Hospital NHS FT found the cyber attack was one of the contributing factors in a patient's death — a long wait for a blood test result. Widely reported as the first UK case where officials publicly linked a cyber attack to a death. Cyber risk in health is clinical risk.

Questions worth asking

“If a key supplier were encrypted tonight, which clinical systems keep working — and what could that supplier's credentials reach beyond the one system they support?” · “Could you show an auditor, from live data, which VLANs an unpatched imaging device can reach today?” · “Which of your community and primary-care sites would drop offline if a single circuit failed?”

Framework mapping — CAF-aligned DSPT

Where the network evidence lands

The CAF-aligned DSPT assesses outcomes, not products, and the judgment belongs to the independent assessor. The network-shaped anchors sit mainly under Objective B — with monitoring under C and impact under D — and NHS England's August 2023 MFA policy gives B2 an unusually concrete edge: MFA must be enforced on all remote user access to all systems, and on all privileged accounts accessing externally hosted systems. Walk this table the same way as the CE mapping on the public-sector page — the fourth column keeps it credible.

Two more healthcare-specific angles

HSCN and internet-first

HSCN (which replaced N3 by November 2020 — ~12,000 sites migrated) remains fully operational, with no announced successor or retirement date. But the direction since the 2018 Internet First policy is towards internet-delivered services, and NHS England's Networks and Connectivity Programme prioritises off-estate workforce connectivity and network modernisation. Position Cato alongside HSCN circuits for internet-first traffic and new sites — never as "the mandated HSCN replacement"; no such mandate exists.

Suppliers: Cyber Essentials Plus

Under PPN 014, NHS bodies require Cyber Essentials in relevant procurements — and NHS Supply Chain went further in September 2025: in-scope suppliers must demonstrate CE Plus (ISO 27001 is not accepted as an alternative), with existing-supplier reviews from 8 September 2025. If the customer is a supplier to the NHS, the CE mapping is their conversation; Key IT Suppliers also land on the CAF-aligned DSPT track.

The Cato solution

A trust estate, segmented and evidenced

A typical trust estate spans an acute hospital, community clinics, GP-style small sites and thousands of remote and roaming clinicians — plus an IoMT population of infusion pumps, imaging modalities and monitors that cannot run agents and often cannot be patched. Cato treats it as one estate: every site and user crosses the same PoP-enforced policy, and east-west separation inside each site is enforced by the LAN Firewall — a global policy under Security → LAN Firewall, scoped per site and VLAN, enforced on the Socket itself. Because enforcement is local to the Socket, clinical-to-device rules keep working even while a site's internet link is down — segmentation that does not depend on the circuit staying up.

Position with care

Say “Cato supports your DSPT work” — never “Cato makes you compliant”. The DSPT is scored across the whole organisation, independent assessors own the judgment, and Objective E belongs to information governance, not the network. For claims about Cato's own certifications, use only the published security, compliance & privacy page.

Sector blueprint

Typical healthcare policies — a starter rulebase

The mapping table stays at outcome level; this is the layer beneath it — an illustrative starter rulebase a trust or large provider could stand up, then shape to its own estate. It is organised the way a DSPT assessor reasons about a clinical network: clinical access allowed explicitly and posture-checked, medical devices confined to the conversations they clinically need, the corporate estate kept off the clinical VLANs except through named integration flows, and suppliers reaching exactly the system they support. None of it certifies anything on its own; it gives the DSPT conversation something concrete to walk.

A starting point — monitor-first, clinically safe

These rules are illustrative, not an exported console. Stand every block rule up in monitor mode first, watch hit counts against real traffic — in a hospital, a mis-scoped block can interrupt a clinical integration, so the monitor phase is a patient-safety step, not a formality. Flip to enforce only after clinical engineering and the integration team have reviewed what would have been blocked. VLAN design, device groups and clinical-risk sign-off are the customer's to decide: Cato supports that work; it does not settle it.

Healthcare scenarios

  • DSPT submission and independent audit. When the auditor arrives, walk this rulebase as the evidence: Device Inventory is the asset picture, the LAN Firewall policy is the segmentation claim as configured, events show rules 2–4 firing on real traffic, and the Audit Trail shows who changed what and when. One console answers the contributing-outcome questions instead of a screenshot hunt across clinical engineering, network and security teams.
  • An imaging vendor needs remote access this week. Stand up rule 6 for the vendor's engineers: a ZTNA segment scoped to the one modality management server, MFA in the path, every session evented. When the work finishes, remove the group — the Audit Trail records both ends. The June 2024 pathology incident is the reference point: a supplier compromise must not become a trust-wide one.
  • Ransomware lands in the corporate estate. A phished back-office laptop starts scanning. Rule 4's corporate-to-clinical block confines it at the Socket — EPR and PACS stay reachable to clinicians while the corporate VLAN is triaged — and because the LAN Firewall enforces locally, containment holds even if responders isolate the site's internet links. The event stream gives the incident team the scan attempts in real time.
  • A new community clinic opens in weeks, not months. Ship an X1600, connect broadband and LTE, and the site inherits the global policy on day one — clinical VLAN separation, MFA'd remote access, filtered patient Wi-Fi — with no dedicated circuit on the critical path. The same pattern covers vaccination sites and pop-up diagnostic hubs, and aligns with the internet-first direction of travel.
