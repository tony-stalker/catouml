Cisco (Umbrella, AnyConnect & SD-WAN) to Cato

A “Cisco SASE” estate is rarely one product — it is Umbrella, AnyConnect on ASA or FTD headends, a Viptela or Meraki overlay and a stack of consoles, each with its own renewal and end-of-life date. This page maps every component to its Cato equivalent and walks a five-phase path — regional eBGP co-existence, parallel pilots, wave cutover with per-site rollback — that lands the whole estate in one platform and one policy.

Business objective

The multi-console problem

Almost no Cisco account runs one product. The typical estate combines Umbrella (its own dashboard, plus virtual appliances on-prem for internal-domain DNS), AnyConnect / Cisco Secure Client terminating on ASA or Firepower headends, Catalyst SD-WAN (Viptela) with the vManage/vSmart/vBond controller stack or Meraki MX with Auto-VPN, ASA/FTD firewalls managed from ASDM, FMC or CDO, and ISE for NAC and VPN posture. Each element carries a separate console, licence model and renewal date — and a separate operational skill set.

Cisco itself is consolidating around the customer: vManage was renamed Catalyst SD-WAN Manager, Umbrella capabilities are being folded into Cisco Secure Access, and Meraki has its own SSE integration path. Customers describe genuine roadmap uncertainty across the overlapping offers — and Secure Access adds yet another console (Security Cloud Control) during any transition.

The objective of this migration is convergence: Sockets on the WAN edge, the global private backbone as the middle mile, and FWaaS, SWG, DNS security, ZTNA, CASB and DLP delivered inline at the PoP — all managed from the Cato Management Application. One platform, one policy model, one renewal.

What maps to what

Component-by-component mapping

The conversion is review-and-map, not lift-and-shift: rule bases shrink when separate DNS, web and firewall policies collapse into one inspection engine at the PoP. This is where years of accumulated Umbrella destination lists and ASA rules get cleaned up rather than copied across.

The migration path

Five phases from multi-console Cisco to one platform

Provenance — read before you present

This page is authored from vendor documentation and Cato PS methodology research; it is not a delivery-verified runbook. Review the design — especially the interconnect, redistribution and rollback details — with Cato Professional Services before committing it to a customer plan.

The arc follows the Cato PS methodology — Export → Review & Map → Deploy → Optimise — applied per estate component, inside a discovery → co-existence → pilot → wave-cutover → decommission programme.

  1. Phase 1 — Discovery and design

    Inventory the whole estate: sites, links and bandwidths; the Viptela topology (hub/spoke regions, OMP/TLOC design, app-route SLA policies buried in vManage templates); Meraki Auto-VPN hubs and spoke modes (split vs full tunnel); the Umbrella policy set (identities, destination lists, content categories, selective-decryption lists); AnyConnect tunnel-groups with their SAML/ISE dependencies; and the ASA/FTD rule bases.

    Capture the business intent hidden in vManage centralised policy and Meraki templates before anything is decommissioned — that is the app-priority and SLA design Cato must reproduce. Map licence co-termination dates (Cisco EA/DNA, Meraki co-term, Umbrella renewal) to build the migration calendar around renewal leverage, and agree success criteria and rollback rules per phase.

  2. Phase 2 — Co-existence foundation: regional interconnect hubs

    Network → Sites

    Stand up the Cato account: admin model, IdP SSO and SCIM provisioning (Entra ID / Okta), and a base policy from Cato best-practice templates. Then build the co-existence fabric: regional Cato interconnect hubs with an eBGP handoff to each region’s Viptela or Meraki hub, keeping traffic symmetric per region.

    At the Viptela hub, redistribute OMP ↔ BGP so legacy-spoke routes reach Cato and Cato-site routes propagate into the overlay. Meraki MX dynamic routing is limited — anchor the handoff at the L3 core switch or firewall behind the MX, not the MX itself. Filter the default route and parallel-site prefixes at the interconnect, and use Cato’s reserved BGP communities to keep migration routes isolated where needed.

  3. Phase 3 — Pilot in parallel

    Security → Internet Firewall

    Pick one to three low-risk, representative sites. Sockets ship zero-touch and self-provision from the Cato Cloud; connect them in parallel (transit VLAN or L3-switch / firewall handoff) and move a subnet at a time, validating WAN paths, internet breakout, voice — and the rollback. Pilot a small user cohort on the Cato Client via MDM, split-tunnel first, alongside the incumbent Secure Client.

    For policy: export Umbrella destination lists to CSV (dashboard download or the Destination Lists API), map content categories and block/allow lists to Cato system categories, custom categories and Internet firewall rules, and recreate DNS-layer controls in DNS Protections. Stage TLS Inspection and IPS in monitor mode before enforcing anything.

  4. Phase 4 — Wave cutover: sites, then users, then DNS

    Access → Client Connectivity Policy

    Sites move in regional waves, keeping each region’s legacy hub as the interconnect until the region completes. Per site: pre-stage the Socket → move the LAN VLANs or default gateway → disable the legacy spoke — OMP withdraws its routes automatically (Meraki: remove the spoke from Auto-VPN) — Cato propagates the prefixes via BGP at the interconnect → validate, then soak with the legacy CPE left racked and configured. Rollback is the mirror image: re-enable the spoke and routing re-converges. Move each site’s Umbrella web/DNS policy to Cato in the same change window — Umbrella network identities are keyed to the site’s public egress IP, which changes to a shared Cato PoP address the moment the Socket goes live. Re-point third-party allowlists keyed to old egress IPs per wave.

    Users follow as AnyConnect → Cato Client cohorts: split-tunnel with the incumbent VPN still available, validate the cohort’s apps, then remove the VPN profile and switch always-on with Client Connectivity Policy and Device Posture profiles recreating the ISE posture rules. DNS cuts over in strict order: sites first, roaming users next (the Client supersedes the Umbrella module), then re-point internal DNS forwarders away from the Umbrella virtual appliances and retire the VAs last — internal-domain handling stays on AD DNS throughout.

  5. Phase 5 — Optimise, then decommission in order

    Monitor → Events

    Tune policy from real events: tighten monitor-mode rules to block, widen TLS Inspection scope, refine app and bandwidth priorities on the backbone. Decommission in sequence — legacy spokes → regional hubs and interconnects → vManage/vSmart/vBond controllers → ASA/FTD headends → Umbrella tenancy at renewal (once event parity is confirmed in CMA) → Meraki MX licences at co-term. Retain ISE for wired NAC and Meraki MS/MR for the LAN where they remain in scope.

Co-existence & rollback

Two overlays, one region at a time

Asymmetry is the enemy. With two overlays joined in more than one place, a flow can exit through one region’s hub and return through another — breaking stateful inspection and dropping traffic. The preferred pattern is regional interconnect hubs with eBGP and AS-path anchoring: cross-region routes carry a longer AS-PATH (the extra inter-hub hop), so each side always prefers its local regional hub — symmetric by construction, with automatic route withdrawal on spoke cutover and simple rollback. It is the same pattern Cato PS has deployed against FortiGate hub estates, applied unchanged to Viptela hubs via OMP ↔ BGP redistribution and to Meraki hub sites via the L3 core.

Migrated — Cato tunnel, DNS/SWG at the PoP Interim — OMP overlay + Umbrella until the wave completes
Regional co-existence: the migrated spoke rides the Cato backbone with DNS and web security enforced at the PoP; the legacy spoke stays on the Viptela overlay and Umbrella. eBGP at the regional interconnect (with OMP ↔ BGP redistribution) keeps routing symmetric, and spoke cutover withdraws routes automatically.
🧭

Symmetric by construction

AS-path anchoring makes each side prefer its local regional hub — no stateful-inspection breakage from cross-region return paths.

↩️

Reversible per site

Cutover is a routing event: disable the spoke and OMP/BGP withdraws the routes; re-enable it and everything converges back.

🛡️

No protection gap

Umbrella keeps covering un-migrated sites and users throughout — nothing about the Cato rollout breaks it while egress IPs are unchanged.

🎛️

One policy at the end

Every wave retires a console. The finish line is a single rule base in CMA for DNS, web, WAN and remote access.

If eBGP at the hub is not possible

  • Parallel connectivity at every site — cleanest, but practical only below roughly 20 sites
  • A single interconnect hub — accept the hairpin latency and migrate one region at a time
  • NAT/PAT at the boundary to force symmetry — low cross-region volumes only; breaks IP-embedding protocols such as LDAP and WMI
  • Customer-managed PBR over GRE/IPsec — complex, generally avoided

Umbrella during the transition

  • Virtual appliances stay until the final DNS repoint — they forward internal domains independently of the WAN path
  • Watch the egress-IP dependency: a site behind Cato no longer matches its Umbrella network identity — move that site’s policy at swap time, not later
  • Existing firewall IPsec/GRE tunnels into Umbrella SIG can keep running in parallel while WAN traffic moves to Cato
  • Let the subscription lapse at renewal once event parity is confirmed in CMA

Rollback rules — agree them before wave one

Sites

Legacy CPE stays racked, powered and configured through an agreed soak window. Rollback = re-enable the spoke; BGP/OMP re-converges automatically.

Users

Rollback = re-enable the AnyConnect profile via MDM. Keep the ASA/FTD headends licensed until the final cohort clears its soak window.

DNS

Keep the Umbrella VAs and tenancy alive until after the last forwarder repoint — rollback is a forwarder change, nothing more.

Gotchas & objections

What bites, and what they’ll say

EIGRP / OSPF meets BGP

Cato’s dynamic routing integration is BGP, and Cisco shops frequently run EIGRP or OSPF in the campus and DC. Plan EIGRP/OSPF ↔ BGP redistribution at the handoff device (or static routes for smaller sites) — and watch administrative-distance and metric-seeding quirks when routes re-enter the IGP.

Meraki is a full-stack dependency

MS switches and MR access points share the MX’s Dashboard and licensing — replacing the MX does not remove either. Scope it as “WAN + security to Cato, Meraki LAN retained”, check co-termination impact, and never plan eBGP on the MX itself — anchor the handoff at the L3 core. Cato’s Meraki Experience Monitoring integration (announced Oct 2025) gives LAN visibility from CMA.

AnyConnect can override Cato Client DNS

Cato documents this conflict directly — and running the Cato Client full-tunnel alongside a third-party VPN is not recommended. Sequence the dual-agent phase: Secure Client for VPN only while the Cato Client runs split-tunnel, or move remote access to Cato first and leave only the Umbrella module until web parity. Audit what is actually installed per cohort first — many estates mix the EOS standalone Roaming Client, the Secure Client module and AnyConnect VPN. Prove it in the pilot.

SAML and ISE posture re-mapping

AnyConnect SAML is configured per tunnel-group on the ASA/FTD — recreate it as Cato IdP SSO (Entra ID, Okta, OneLogin; multiple IdPs supported) with SCIM keeping groups in sync. ISE posture rules map to Device Posture + Client Connectivity Policy — but wired 802.1X, RADIUS CoA and switch-port enforcement stay with ISE. Set that expectation in week one, not in the eleventh hour.

Umbrella’s policy model is not Cato’s

Umbrella evaluates identity precedence (roaming > AD user/group > internal network > network); Cato is an ordered rule base with users, groups and sites as sources. Selective-decryption lists become TLS Inspection rules. Verify niche behaviours — block-page bypass codes, for one — during Review & Map rather than assuming 1:1 parity. And inventory everything keyed to office egress IPs (SaaS allowlists, partner firewalls) in discovery.

“We’ll just move to Secure Access”

A fair objection — with three counters. That path is itself a migration (new SKUs, new console, an agent swap, a router refresh), not a renewal. It preserves the multi-product estate — SD-WAN, SSE, firewalls and NAC remain separate stacks. And Cato collapses the same scope into one platform and one policy model, with the co-existence patterns above de-risking the transition.
