iboss Proxy to Cato

iboss estates are proxy-heritage: PAC files, per-platform connector agents, GRE/IPsec tunnels and SaaS allowlists keyed to dedicated egress IPs — most common in education and government, and usually running beside a separate network vendor because iboss was SSE-only until 2024. This page maps every iboss component to its Cato equivalent and lays out a wave-based steering cutover that keeps PAC as the rollback rail throughout.

Business objective

Why estates move off iboss

Read this first — research, not a delivery-verified runbook

This page is authored from sourced vendor and Cato documentation research, not from a completed customer migration. Validate the plan with Cato Professional Services before using it in a customer engagement.

iboss began life as an appliance web filter for education and grew into a cloud security platform with a distinctive pitch: single-tenant, containerised gateways per customer, each egressing from dedicated source IPs. It is strongest in US K-12, libraries and government, where CIPA-compliance messaging and FedRAMP Moderate carry weight. Gartner's 2024 SSE Magic Quadrant placed it as a Niche Player — crediting aggressive availability and latency SLAs and low pricing, while flagging weak SaaS/API integrations, no SSPM and a customer base concentrated in North America. Peer reviews are broadly positive (4.8/5 across 131 reviews at research time) — treat individual complaints as anecdotes to probe, not blanket claims.

The structural driver is architectural: iboss was an SSE-only platform until it announced Zero Trust SD-WAN in May 2024. Estates that also need WAN transformation — MPLS replacement, site-to-site optimisation, a global backbone — have historically run iboss alongside a separate network vendor: two policy planes, two support paths, two renewals. Cato converges networking and security on one cloud-native platform in a single console, so an SD-WAN or firewall refresh, an MPLS expiry or an education re-procurement cycle is the moment a "filter renewal" becomes a platform consolidation conversation.

The second driver is operational: iboss steering leans on PAC files, explicit proxy settings and per-platform connectors. Years of PAC exceptions and bypass lists encode tribal knowledge that someone has to keep maintaining. Cato steers transparently — the Cato Client for roaming devices, a Socket or IPsec tunnel for sites — with no proxy configuration on the endpoint at all.

What you're migrating

Four steering paths and one IP dependency

An iboss migration is first and foremost a steering migration. iboss documents four ways traffic reaches its cloud, and a mature estate usually runs several at once. Each one needs its own inventory, its own cutover mechanic and its own rollback lever.

Cloud connector agents Most common

Installed on managed devices: redirects traffic, authenticates the user, maps group policy and installs the iboss root MITM certificate for TLS inspection. Chromebooks are covered via a Chrome extension — which matters enormously in education fleets.

PAC files / explicit proxy Browser-level

Customer-hosted PAC scripts, distributed by GPO/MDM, proxy to iboss off-network and route direct or via local gateways on-network. The PAC and its exception list are both a migration liability and — kept alive — the cheapest rollback lever in the project.

GRE / IPsec tunnels Sites

From sites or SD-WAN edges, terminating on the public IPs of the customer's iboss gateway nodes — typically carrying guest, BYOD and IoT traffic that can't run an agent.

DNS redirection Lightweight

DNS-based filtering for zones where nothing heavier is deployed. Easy to forget in discovery, easy to leave orphaned after cutover — enumerate the zones explicitly.

Then there is the signature iboss feature: dedicated cloud source IPs. Because gateways are single-tenant, every customer egresses from IPs unique to them — and many have keyed SaaS tenant restrictions, exam/assessment platform allowlists and partner firewall rules to those IPs. Every one of those references must be found and dual-listed with Cato allocated IPs before any wave moves. Missing one is the classic "the migration broke X" incident.

Ask yourself

"Which SaaS tenant restrictions, exam platforms and partner firewalls are keyed to your iboss dedicated IPs today — and does anyone own that list?" If the answer is a shrug, Phase 0 starts there.

Qualify before you commit — two documented gaps

Chromebook fleets: iboss covers Chromebooks via a Chrome extension, but ChromeOS does not appear in Cato's documented Client OS list (Windows, macOS, Linux, iOS, Android). For Chromebook-heavy schools this is the first technical qualification question — agree the supported approach with Cato PS/product before committing a migration plan.

Google Workspace identity: Cato documents SCIM provisioning for Entra ID, Okta and OneLogin — Google Workspace is not on that list. Google-identity schools need an agreed identity design (SSO and provisioning path) validated in discovery. Do not hand-wave either of these.

The Cato solution

Replace the steering, keep a way back

The pattern that de-risks the whole programme is wave-based steering cutover with PAC as the fallback rail. Both root CAs are deployed side by side before anything moves, so TLS inspection works in either direction. Then cohorts cut over per OU, year group or school — each device wholly on one vendor at a time, with the agent swap done atomically per device via MDM. The iboss PAC stays hosted and unchanged throughout: rollback for a browser-steered cohort is a single GPO/MDM change re-pointing to the old PAC.

Migration path

Five phases from PAC sprawl to PoP breakout

Anchored to Cato's published guidance — phased migration, pilot first, no big-bang cutover, with Cato and partner Professional Services assisting policy conversion. Timings are indicative for a mid-size estate; size Phase 3 to the number of schools/sites.

  1. Phase 0 — Discover the steering estate weeks 1–2

    Enumerate every path into iboss: PAC URLs and their GPO/MDM distribution scope, connector fleet by OS (including the Chromebook extension count), GRE/IPsec tunnels per site, DNS-redirection zones, and any proxy chaining from on-prem gateways. Export the policy base — categories, allow/block lists, per-group overrides, SSL bypass lists, CASB/DLP rules, ZTNA app definitions — and plan to re-author, not import. Build the dependency register: every SaaS tenant restriction and third-party allowlist keyed to iboss dedicated IPs, and anything pinned to the iboss root CA. Harvest the PAC and bypass lists as the seed for Cato TLS-inspection bypasses — the cheapest policy-mining asset in the project. Capture identity (Entra ID, Google Workspace, AD) and the per-OU policy tiers, then fix the Cato design inputs: PoP selection, Socket vs IPsec per site, allocated-IP count, TLS inspection scope.

  2. Phase 1 — Build co-existence before touching steering weeks 2–4

    Stand up the Cato account: sites, SCIM/identity integration, baseline Internet firewall and TLS inspection policy from Cato best practices, Content Restrictions for education tenants, and the recommended CASB/DLP starter policy. The critical move: push the Cato root certificate to all endpoints alongside the existing iboss root CA before any steering change — Cato documents the certificate as mandatory for TLS inspection, downloadable from the CMA and deployable via MDM. Removing the iboss CA comes last in the project, not first. Define which cohorts and sites steer where, and the rollback lever for each.

  3. Phase 2 — Pilot one site and one agent cohort weeks 4–8

    Pick a pilot that exercises the risky bits: one school/site with a tunnel — replaced by a Socket (preferred, adds HA/QoS) or IPsec to the nearest PoP — plus one roaming-agent cohort, IT staff first. Swap agents atomically per device via MDM: uninstall/disable the iboss connector and deploy the Cato Client in the same maintenance window per device ring. Never run both agents steering simultaneously — two TLS-intercepting redirection agents create hard-to-diagnose breakage. Validate category parity on a test URL list, TLS inspection, SafeSearch/YouTube enforcement, ZTNA app access, user-level reporting in the CMA, and SaaS logins from the new allocated egress IPs.

  4. Phase 3 — Wave cutover per OU / school weeks 8–16

    Cut over in waves aligned to the existing iboss group-policy tiers — per OU, year group or school — so filtering levels move with the cohort and safeguarding staff can verify each wave. Before each wave moves: add Cato allocated IPs to the wave's SaaS tenant restrictions and allowlists (run both vendors' IPs in parallel through the transition), re-point PAC-configured browsers to direct/Client steering, and retire the wave's tunnels. Re-point SIEM export and safeguarding/monitoring integrations as each wave completes.

  5. Phase 4 — Optimise, then decommission in order weeks 16+

    Tune policy from live CMA analytics, tighten TLS-inspection bypasses, and switch on the remaining services — RBI on uncategorised sites, DLP content profiles. Then decommission in strict order: (1) disable iboss steering — PAC distribution, connectors, tunnels; (2) remove iboss dedicated IPs from SaaS allowlists once logs show no residual use; (3) remove the iboss root CA from endpoints via MDM; (4) revoke iboss directory and SIEM integrations; (5) terminate the subscription only after archiving the full reporting-retention window — safeguarding logs are statutory evidence in education.

Co-existence & rollback

Every wave keeps a way back

PAC as the fallback rail

Keep the iboss PAC hosted and unchanged for the whole project. Rollback for any browser-steered cohort is then a single GPO/MDM change re-pointing to the old PAC — no re-install, no re-configuration.

Agent cohorts

Rollback = re-enable the iboss connector profile and remove the Cato Client via the same MDM ring used for deployment. Keep the iboss tenant licensed and its policies change-frozen until each wave is signed off.

Sites

Run the Cato Socket in-line while leaving the legacy tunnel-to-iboss configured but down. Rollback is re-enabling the tunnel and re-pointing the default route.

Certificates

Both root CAs are deployed side by side from Phase 1, so TLS inspection works in either direction during co-existence. CA removal is deferred to decommission — rollback never breaks HTTPS.

Never split one device between both clouds

Per-user, per-OU waves keep each device wholly on one vendor at a time. Avoid any design where iboss handles some categories and Cato others for the same endpoint — two TLS-intercepting chains on one device is a support nightmare, not a migration strategy.

Success gates per wave: helpdesk ticket rate against baseline, category-parity spot checks, safeguarding-report continuity, and SaaS login success from the new egress IPs. A wave isn't done until all four hold.

Objection handling

Gotchas and objections

"iboss gives us CIPA-grade education filtering"

Cato documents Content Restrictions with SafeSearch and YouTube Moderate/Strict enforcement — demo it, and be upfront about the documented caveat: browsers using DNS-over-HTTPS bypass Content Restrictions, so DoH must be controlled via browser policy. For fine-grained Google-service controls, run a feature-parity check against the customer's actual iboss ruleset rather than asserting parity. UK schools: map requirements to DfE filtering-and-monitoring standards, not US CIPA language.

Per-user reporting expectations

Education and government customers use iboss reporting as safeguarding and compliance evidence. Show CMA user-level analytics and SIEM export during the pilot — and archive the iboss reporting history before terminating the subscription. Reporting continuity is a success gate, not an afterthought.

SaaS allowlists keyed to dedicated egress IPs

Every tenant restriction, partner firewall rule and exam-platform allowlist keyed to iboss IPs must be found and dual-listed with Cato allocated IPs before cutover. The default Cato licence includes 3 allocated IPs — size this early if the customer needs regional egress or more IPs.

"iboss is single-vendor SASE now too"

Its SD-WAN launched in May 2024, versus Cato's converged SD-WAN-plus-security platform operating since the mid-2010s and validated across the single-vendor SASE market. Probe the maturity of the SD-WAN estate: routing features, edge hardware ecosystem, global private backbone, last-mile HA.

"Per-tenant containers are more secure"

Acknowledge the architecture, then reframe on outcomes: what matters is inspection quality, policy convergence and operations. Gartner's caution that iboss depth in SaaS/API security lags is the counterweight to the container story.

Latency and SLA comparison

iboss publishes seven-nines availability and a 100 ms latency SLA. Do not counter with invented numbers — position Cato's PoP proximity, private backbone and published uptime commitments, and prove latency empirically in the pilot.

Sector blueprint

Typical education policies — a starter rulebase

The mapping and phased path above get you to a translated policy; this is what a good one looks like once it lands. The worked before→after translation of a single Students/Staff tier lives on the iboss Policy Migration page — this section is the fuller destination blueprint: a starter Cato rulebase for a school or multi-academy trust, the shape most education estates converge on once the per-OU tiers are rationalised.

It is a starting grid, not a drop-in. Cohorts — year groups, phases, staff tiers, the sixth form — become SCIM groups; the rules sit in one ordered Internet firewall policy, most-specific cohort first; and every rule below lands in monitor for its OU wave before any block or prompt goes live, the same monitor-first discipline as the steering cutover.

Illustrative starter blueprint — shape it to the safeguarding policy

The rulebase below is illustrative, not a compliance template. Deploy it monitor-first, one OU wave at a time, and align every decision to the institution's own safeguarding and acceptable-use policy and to the filtering-and-monitoring expectations UK schools work to — KCSIE-style safeguarding duties and the DfE filtering-and-monitoring standards — as design intent, not a statutory checklist. The safeguarding lead signs off the category and prompt/block choices, not just IT.

Education scenarios

  • Safeguarding incident review. When a concern is raised, the designated safeguarding lead needs a per-user trail fast — Monitor → Events filtered to one pupil across web, search and blocked-category hits, exportable and retained. The Event tracking on every rule above is what makes that trail exist; it is reporting continuity carried across from iboss, not a nice-to-have.
  • Exam-season lockdown. For the exam window the exam-cohort group flips to the allowlist-only rule — assessment platforms permitted, everything else blocked — then reverts afterwards. One scoped rule change per window, with the block already proven in monitor so no candidate meets a surprise deny mid-exam.
  • New academy joining a trust. Onboarding a school into a multi-academy trust is policy inheritance, not a rebuild: add the site/Socket, map the new OUs onto the trust's existing SCIM groups, and this ordered rulebase applies on day one, with local exceptions layered as scoped rules above it. One policy plane across every academy instead of a filter tenant per school.
  • Filtering-review evidence. An Ofsted-style filtering-and-monitoring review — or a governor/DSL audit — asks the school to show what is filtered, for whom, and how incidents are recorded. The CMA answers it live: the ordered rulebase is the "what and for whom", user-attributed events are the "how recorded" — evidence on screen rather than a vendor report request.
