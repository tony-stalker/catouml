Zscaler (ZIA / ZPA) to Cato

ZIA and ZPA leave the customer running two products, two policy sets and an agent that only does security — with the WAN still on somebody else's kit. This use case walks the proven path onto the Cato SASE Cloud: a four-phase policy conversion, interim architectures that keep ZIA reachable while you migrate, and dual-agent co-existence that de-risks the endpoint cutover — grounded in a real customer migration with the lessons learned left in.

Business objective

Why organisations move off Zscaler

Zscaler customers run ZIA for internet security, ZPA for private access, and Zscaler Client Connector (ZCC) on every endpoint — while the WAN itself lives on separate SD-WAN or MPLS estate behind legacy firewalls. Every one of those boundaries is a policy set to maintain, a console to check and a gap where visibility ends.

The long-term strategic goal of this migration is a simplified, standard solution: one unified platform for security and networking, end-to-end visibility of every flow, and direct internet breakout from the servicing Cato PoP with SD-WAN preference routing — instead of hairpinning traffic into a separate security cloud.

The migration itself is the objection you have to beat. The rest of this page is the answer: convert policy methodically, run both clouds in parallel, and cut over one cohort at a time.

Policy conversion

Four phases from ZIA / ZPA policy to Cato policy

Cato Professional Services runs policy conversion as a four-phase process. It is deliberately not a lift-and-shift: years of accumulated Zscaler rules get cleaned up, refactored and gap-analysed against Cato best practices on the way across.

1 · Export ZIA + ZPA

ZIA: policies, URL filtering, firewall, web security, DLP, Cloud App policies, Advanced Threat Protection, SSL inspection, bandwidth control, and user/group settings.

ZPA: segment groups, access policies, Browser Access, Client Forwarding, inspection policies, trusted networks, server groups and posture profiles.

2 · Review & map Clean-up

ZIA: map to the Cato Internet firewall, App Control (tenant restrictions, CASB, DLP), Threat Prevention, SCIM groups and TLS Inspection.

ZPA: review groups and site hosts, then map to the WAN firewall and access control. Dead rules and duplicates are refactored out here, not copied across.

3 · Deploy Best practices

ZIA: implement Cato best practices — category and service restrictions, firewall, TLS Inspection, CASB, and DLP monitoring policies.

ZPA: WAN firewall, segmentation and access control best practices, Browser Access, trusted networks, and group/host configuration.

4 · Optimise Ongoing

Event analysis and performance tuning once real traffic is flowing: adjust policy restrictions where they bite, and enhance security through tighter segmentation. This is also where the gap analysis against Cato best practices pays off — the converted estate ends up cleaner than the one it replaced.

Ask yourself

“If you exported your ZIA and ZPA policy base today, how much of it could you still explain — and how much would you actually want to carry forward?” The honest answer is why conversion starts with clean-up, not copy.

The Cato solution

Co-exist first, cut over in phases

The pattern that de-risks the whole programme is dual-agent co-existence: ZCC and the Cato Client run side by side on the same endpoint throughout the migration. On-site, the ZCC tunnel simply rides inside the Cato socket tunnel; when remote, the ZCC and Cato SDP tunnels co-exist on the workstation. Internet protection never has a gap, and either agent can be backed out independently at any phase.

On the network side, Cato PoPs keep ZIA reachable during transition: multiple active IKEv2 IPsec tunnels from the Cato cloud to ZIA, load-balanced by source/destination, egressing from the Cato PoP closest to Zscaler over the Tier-1 backbone. Sites and users move onto Cato for transport and WAN security first — internet policy flips to direct PoP breakout only once it has been converted and proven.

Interim architectures

Four ways to keep ZIA reachable during transition

Pick per site, not per customer — most migrations mix these. The first two bridge internet traffic to ZIA while Cato takes over transport; the second two phase Cato into sites that keep their existing firewall running in parallel.

IKEv2 multiple active tunnels PoP → ZIA

  • Cato builds parallel IPsec tunnels straight to ZIA — the deck's worked example runs 3 active + 3 backup
  • Backhaul load-balanced by source / destination
  • Egress from the closest Cato PoP to Zscaler, over the Tier-1 backbone
  • Support for multiple interconnects
  • Explicitly a temporary ZIA interim solution

Internet backhaul to existing FW Site FW → ZIA

  • Internet breakout stays at existing sites during transition
  • Reuses the firewall's proven GRE / IPsec connectivity into ZIA — nothing new to build on the Zscaler side
  • Load-balancing and site redundancy across breakout sites
  • Backhaul load-balanced by source / destination
  • Temporary ZIA interim solution

FW VLAN termination Phased replacement

  • Existing firewall keeps its VLANs and parallel ZIA connectivity
  • Cato joins as a transit network — BGP or static routing
  • Site ranges land as temporary routed ranges: usable in Cato policy now, converted to VLANs at firewall retirement
  • Simple bypass of agent connectivity
  • No Cato bandwidth or commercial impact

Cato VLAN termination Phased replacement

  • The socket terminates local VLANs — Cato-managed inter-VLAN routing and visibility from day one
  • Firewall DMZ handoff carries the socket's WAN uplink
  • Internet traffic bypasses the Cato WAN and still breaks out from the firewall during transition
  • Reuses existing local firewall ZIA connectivity
  • No Cato bandwidth or commercial impact
Field evidence

Case study: a UK professional-services firm

Starting point

  • UK-headquartered professional-services firm
  • Zscaler ZIA for internet — GRE tunnels at sites plus ZCC on managed endpoints, with identity
  • Not using ZPA; heavy focus on ZTNA as the goal
  • MPLS WAN protected by legacy firewalls
  • Remote access on a legacy remote-access VPN

Why they moved

  • Extremely unhappy with the Zscaler solution
  • Purchased Zscaler CASB — but it was not working for them
  • Wanted the WAN protected with a ZTNA model, not just internet security
  • Bought Cato as a Zscaler ZIA replacement

The phased cutover

  1. Phase 0 — WAN first, internet untouched

    Sites stayed on ZIA GRE tunnels and endpoints on ZCC. Cato Sockets were deployed and WAN protection built with user awareness before anyone moved off Zscaler: policy-based routing steered WAN traffic to Cato while the default route (internet) still pointed at ZIA.

  2. Phase 1 — SDP to every user

    Cato Client (SDP) rolled out to all users with a split tunnel for RFC 1918 over Cato — not always-on, manually enabled when remote. Internet stayed on ZIA at sites and on ZCC when remote: ZCC and SDP co-existing on the same workstations, dual tunnels while remote.

  3. Phase 2 — Default route to Cato for WAN

    WAN rules completed on the ZTNA model, enforced purely on Cato identities. PBR was removed and the default route migrated to Cato for WAN access; non-user systems (servers, IoT) moved to Cato SWG via the socket on a minimal rule set, keeping visibility and posture. ZIA still protected internet via ZCC — its tunnel now riding inside the Cato socket tunnel on-site.

  4. Phase 3 — Copy SWG policies, prove on a test cohort

    Zscaler SWG policies were copied into Cato SWG. A subset of users had ZCC removed in an on-prem sandbox to confirm functionality — deliberately isolating SDP policy dependencies from the test methodology.

  5. Phase 4 — Always-on SDP, big-bang split-tunnel removal

    SDP switched to always-on (still split-tunnelling RFC 1918, still leaning on ZCC for ZIA), then the split tunnel was removed in one move: SDP became the default route to the internet via the Cato PoP, with ZCC forming its ZIA tunnel through Cato to the Zscaler PoP. ZCC was then removed at convenience — internet traffic flipping from ZIA to Cato SWG per endpoint.

Lessons learned — keep these in the room

This migration worked, but it was not friction-free. SEs who tell this part of the story honestly build more trust than any polished slide.

Unidentified users broke SWG logic

All SDP users were being identified as the “All Unidentified Users” group while user-group logic worked correctly behind the socket — causing SWG policy logic failures. A flaw in Cato's logic (since being fixed), quickly worked around with an exception for the All SDP Users group.

TLS inspection vs Outlook

TLSi caused issues with Outlook, and the customer's change management delayed diagnostic testing — a little over a month of rollout delay. Root cause sat on the customer side and was resolved via a Cato support ticket.

Change windows set the pace

Tight change control and narrow windows made progress extremely slow. Agree the change-window cadence for every phase before the project starts — the technology was never the bottleneck.

Looking forward: integrating the recently released agent-based SDP identity with WMI-based identity to improve the user-awareness experience, and using user/group-based split-tunnel policies (as they release) to simplify Phase-3 test cohorts and make the Phase-4 cutover gradual instead of big-bang.
