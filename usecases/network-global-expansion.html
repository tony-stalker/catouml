Global Expansion & China Connectivity

When the business opens in a new region, the network is usually the last thing to arrive — carrier contracts, shipped firewalls and staging engineers turn a market entry into a quarters-long project. This use case shows how a new site lands on the nearest of 80+ Cato PoPs with the full security stack and WAN optimisation from day one — and how China, the hardest case of all, is handled compliantly through Cato's in-country presence with licensed local partners.

Business objective

Why this matters

Expansion is a board-level event: a new sales office in Madrid, a plant in South Africa, a joint venture in Sydney, a manufacturing partner in China. The commercial team signs the lease in weeks — and then discovers that the network needs carrier circuits ordered months in advance, firewalls purchased, staged and shipped, and an engineer on a plane to rack it all. IT becomes the reason the site opens late.

The objective: make the network a non-event in every market entry — connected, secured and visible on day one, with no infrastructure project per country.

The problem

The traditional new-region playbook is a project per country

Every new region traditionally restarts the same build: source MPLS or business internet from a carrier you have never worked with, or extend a DIY VPN overlay — then procure, stage and ship the security stack to sit behind it. Each step has its own lead time, its own local dependency, and its own way of slipping.

Sourcing the connectivity

  • MPLS and international circuits: long carrier lead times, per-country contracts
  • Local-loop delivery depends on providers you can't escalate
  • DIY VPN overlay rides the public internet — no predictability on long haul
  • Every region adds another routing and QoS design to maintain

Shipping the security stack

  • Firewalls purchased, licensed and staged before they ship
  • Export, import and customs logistics for network hardware
  • An engineer travels to rack, cable and cut over the site
  • Policy rebuilt per site — and it drifts from HQ from day one

The extreme case

China: where the DIY playbook breaks down

China concentrates every expansion problem into one country. Cross-border enterprise connectivity is regulated, so the connectivity itself has a compliance dimension the rest of the world doesn't. And the common DIY pattern — a VPN overlay from the China office back to Europe or the US over the public internet — suffers on both fronts: performance through congested international gateways is unpredictable, and an unlicensed overlay carries real regulatory risk for the business running it.

Cato's answer is deliberately simple to state: Cato provides compliant connectivity into and out of China through its in-country presence, operated with licensed local partners, bringing Chinese sites onto the Cato global private backbone. The site gets the same experience as everywhere else — same policy, same visibility, same console — with the regulatory side handled by partners licensed to provide it.

Positioning note — keep the China story precise

State it exactly as above: compliant connectivity into and out of China via Cato's PoPs and licensed local partners. Scope the specifics — cities, licensing detail, service options and commercials — with the Cato China team per opportunity. Don't improvise them in the room.

The Cato solution

The network is already there — the site just joins it

With Cato, expansion stops being a build and becomes an on-boarding. The backbone of 80+ PoPs is the infrastructure — it already exists in the target region, run by Cato, with the full security stack and WAN optimisation in every PoP. Opening a site means choosing an on-ramp: ship a zero-touch socket that anyone who can plug in power and WAN can install, or start on day one with the Cato Client or an IPsec tunnel while the hardware is in transit. The moment the site connects to its nearest PoP, it inherits the same policy and the same visibility as every other site in the estate.

Live site traffic — nearest-PoP on-ramp, full stack inherited instantly
A growing footprint: the London estate plus Madrid (zero-touch socket), Sydney (IPsec from day one) and a new plant on the Cato Client while its socket ships — each lands on its nearest PoP under the same policy. China joins compliantly via a licensed local partner.

Three on-ramps, one outcome

Zero-touch socket

The socket ships direct to the site. Anyone who can plug in power and a WAN cable installs it — it calls home, is claimed to its site in the CMA and pulls its configuration. No staging, no engineer on a plane.

Client & IPsec on day one

The landing team works from day one on the Cato Client; an existing router, firewall or cloud edge joins over IPsec — exactly how the Sydney site (aws-sydney-ipsec) runs in a demonstration environment.

China via licensed partners

Compliant connectivity into and out of China through Cato's in-country presence with licensed local partners, onto the same private backbone — instead of a DIY VPN with performance and compliance risk.
