Microsoft DirectAccess to Cato

Microsoft has deprecated DirectAccess and will remove it from a future Windows Server release — so every estate still running it has to move anyway. This is the focused remote-access play: replace DirectAccess and the infrastructure that keeps it alive — the Network Location Server, the PKI, the IP-HTTPS gateway and the Group Policy plumbing — with the Cato Client and SDP. Always-on like DirectAccess, but cross-platform, no domain-join requirement, with device posture, IdP MFA and app-level least privilege enforced at the nearest PoP, and nothing left to keep alive.

Business objective

The forced decision point

Microsoft has made the decision for you. Its guidance is unambiguous: "DirectAccess has been deprecated and will be removed in a future version of Windows Server," and it "highly recommends that you use Always On VPN instead of DirectAccess for new deployments." DirectAccess still ships and still functions in Windows Server 2025 and other supported releases — but it is on the removal path, and the countdown is your Windows Server refresh cycle. When the estate moves to a release without DirectAccess, remote access stops being a feature you can renew.

So the question is not whether to run this project — it is what you land on. Microsoft's in-place successor, Always On VPN, is the sanctioned Windows path, but it is still a VPN gateway estate you build and operate yourself: RRAS gateways, NPS/RADIUS, a certificate authority, HA pairs. DirectAccess had deeper structural limits than any of that fixes. It is Windows-only and domain-joined-only — Microsoft states it "provides support only for domain-joined clients," and only on Enterprise editions of Windows, so Macs, phones, tablets and contractor laptops were never covered. It is transparent but fragile: the Network Location Server is a single point of failure, IP-HTTPS carries a performance cost, and the whole thing rests on a PKI and Group Policy estate that has to be renewed and nursed. And it grants network-level access, not least-privilege ZTNA.

Cato SDP inverts the model. The Cato Client is always-on like DirectAccess, but it runs on Windows, macOS and mobile, needs no domain join, authenticates through your IdP with SSO and MFA, and checks device posture continuously. It terminates at the nearest of Cato's PoPs — where the full security stack is enforced in the cloud — and reaches datacentre and cloud applications directly over the global private backbone, at the level of the named application rather than the subnet. There is no NLS, no PKI estate, no IP-HTTPS gateway to keep alive. Same forced migration, fundamentally better end state.

A deprecation with a moving deadline

DirectAccess is deprecated today and remains available in Windows Server 2025, so it is easy to defer. But it will be removed from a future release, and the trigger is your own server-estate refresh — not a date you control. Planning the move now is cheaper than discovering the feature is gone during an OS upgrade.

The Windows-only, domain-joined ceiling

DirectAccess supports only domain-joined clients on Enterprise editions of Windows. Every Mac, every phone, every tablet and every contractor or BYOD laptop sits outside it — served, if at all, by a second remote-access tool. The population DirectAccess never reached is exactly the one that has grown.

Transparent, but fragile

The Network Location Server tells the client whether it is inside or outside the network — and when it is unreachable, clients misjudge their location and connectivity breaks, which makes it a single point of failure. IP-HTTPS, the fallback that tunnels through firewalls, adds an encryption overhead; and the PKI and GPO estate underneath all needs renewing and nursing.

Network access, not least privilege

DirectAccess connects the machine to the corporate network — full intranet reach governed by whatever firewall rules kept pace. There is no per-application gate and no continuous device-posture decision. Once the tunnel is up, lateral reach is the network's, not the policy's.

Questions worth asking

How many non-Windows and unmanaged users are unserved by DirectAccess today — Macs, phones, contractors — and what tool covers them instead? What happens to remote clients when the NLS is down? Who owns the certificate authority and the renewal calendar? And which Windows Server release in your estate is the one that finally forces the change?

The Cato solution

From DA gateway to nearest PoP

The Cato Client is a lightweight, always-on agent that connects each user to the nearest Cato PoP, authenticating through your IdP (Entra ID, Okta, OneLogin — multiple IdPs supported) with SSO and MFA, and evaluating device posture continuously throughout the session rather than only at connect. From the PoP, traffic to private applications rides the global private backbone to the datacentre Socket; internet and SaaS traffic breaks out through the same inspection stack that protects sites. There is no DirectAccess gateway, no Network Location Server, no certificate authority dedicated to remote access and no IP-HTTPS listener — nothing to size, renew or nurse.

The migration path

Six steps from DirectAccess to SDP

The sequencing below follows Cato's recommended practice for always-on client migrations: build the Cato side first, move cohorts cleanly, and keep the DirectAccess gateway warm until the last wave clears — then decommission the plumbing deliberately.

  1. Discover & inventory

    Map what DirectAccess actually encodes: the client GPOs that push the configuration, the Network Location Server, the certificate authority and certificate templates it depends on, the IP-HTTPS gateway, and the DA server roles. Confirm who is in scope — and explicitly capture the users DirectAccess never covered: Mac, mobile, unmanaged and contractor. That non-Windows population is part of the target from the start, not an afterthought.

  2. Build the Cato side

    Access → Device Posture Access → Client Connectivity Policy

    Connect the IdP with SSO and SCIM provisioning so users and groups stay in sync. Recreate the "managed corporate device" intent as Device Posture profiles — certificate present, disk encryption, anti-malware, OS version, domain membership where wanted — and run them in monitor first. Build the Client Connectivity Policy (who may connect, from where, on what device) and the app-level access rules that replace network-level intranet reach.

  3. Pilot cohort — swap, don't stack

    DirectAccess installs its own VPN tunnel adapter, and it does not tolerate a second always-on tunnel beside it: leave DirectAccess and the Cato Client both active on one device and their adapters and routing tables fight, producing intermittent, hard-to-diagnose connectivity. So the pilot is a per-device swap, not a parallel run — remove the DirectAccess client configuration for the device (GPO / unenrol) so its tunnel adapter tears down, then the Cato Client becomes the sole tunnel. Validate on that device: always-on connects, every in-scope application is reachable, and posture verdicts are correct before you widen the ring.

  4. Wave rollout

    Roll cohorts by department or region. For each, remove the DirectAccess client GPO (or unenrol the device) so the Cato Client becomes the primary path, and run the same acceptance checklist. Crucially, extend each wave to the Mac, mobile and contractor users DirectAccess never reached — the same policy now covers the whole workforce, not just domain-joined Windows.

  5. Decommission the plumbing

    Once the last cohort is off, retire the DirectAccess gateway, the Network Location Server and the IP-HTTPS listener, and release the certificate-authority role and templates dedicated to remote access. Re-point anything externally keyed to the DA egress addresses as part of the wave plan. This is the estate DirectAccess needed that now simply disappears.

  6. Optimise

    Monitor → Events

    Flip Device Posture from monitor to enforce, keep always-on so users are protected before they think about connecting, and tighten access from network-level reach to app-level rules using real traffic evidence from Events. This is where the estate ends up ahead of where an Always On VPN rebuild would have left it — cross-platform, posture-aware and least-privilege.

Co-existence & rollback

Cohort mechanics, GPO as the lever

Client migrations co-exist by cohort, not on the device. At any moment during the rollout some users run DirectAccess and some run the Cato Client — but never both on the same endpoint at the same time: their tunnel adapters conflict and the user gets unpredictable connectivity. The swap is therefore atomic per device, and the lever that moves a cohort — and the lever that rolls it back — is the same: the DirectAccess client GPO.

One tunnel at a time

Do not run DirectAccess and the Cato Client together on one device — the two always-on tunnel adapters clash and produce intermittent, hard-to-diagnose behaviour. Removing the DirectAccess client configuration must happen as the Cato Client takes over, not after.

Cohort mechanics

Cohorts follow deployment rings: pilot → early adopters → departmental waves. Per device, remove the DirectAccess GPO so its tunnel adapter tears down as the Cato Client takes over as the sole tunnel — never both at once — with a per-cohort acceptance checklist of the apps that cohort actually uses.

GPO revert = rollback

Rollback is mechanical and per cohort: re-apply the DirectAccess client GPO and the device is back on the old path at next policy refresh. Because the DA gateway stays up until the final wave, the known-good route is always there to fall back to.

Keep the gateway warm

Rollback stays cheap only while the old path exists. Keep the DirectAccess gateway, the NLS and the CA running and monitored until the last cohort completes its soak window — then decommission deliberately, not opportunistically.

Rollback rule

Do not retire the Network Location Server, the certificate authority or the IP-HTTPS gateway until the final cohort has cleared. They are the fallback path; pulling them early turns a routine rollback into an incident.
