Offshore Development Centres & BPO Access

An offshore development centre or BPO floor puts thousands of developers and agents — many on outsourcer-owned or unmanaged devices, with constant joiner/leaver churn — inside your code repositories, CRM and VDI. At the same time, regulators and end customers demand the data stays in-country and off the endpoint. This use case shows how Cato combines Client and Clientless ZTNA, RBI and CASB/DLP with a private backbone that keeps VDI usable from the other side of the world.

Business objective

Why this matters

Offshore delivery is now core to how software gets built and customers get served: development centres and contact-centre floors run by outsourcing partners, staffed at scale, often a continent away from the systems they work in. Those teams need deep access — Git repositories and CI pipelines for developers, CRM and VDI for agents — yet they are the population the business controls least: devices owned by the outsourcer or the individual, security posture unknown, and turnover so high that this quarter's access list is already out of date.

Meanwhile the pressure runs the other way. Data-protection regulators and the enterprise's own customers write it into contracts: customer records and source code must stay in-country, inside approved applications, and never persist on the endpoint. The objective is to give every offshore developer and agent fast access to exactly the applications their role requires — while being able to prove that nothing sensitive ever left them.

The challenge

Scale, churn and sovereignty — on someone else's devices

The offshore reality

  • Thousands of developers and agents across multiple delivery partners
  • Devices owned by the outsourcer or the individual — not by you
  • High staff turnover: joiners and leavers every week, per site
  • Deep access required: repos, CI/CD, CRM, VDI, ticketing
  • Contracts and regulators demand in-country, off-endpoint data handling

The legacy answer — and why it creaks

  • VPN concentrators grant network-level access to unmanaged devices
  • VDI farms bolted on as the data-control workaround — for every task
  • Long-haul public internet between the floor and the VDI farm: lag, jitter, drops
  • Screen latency makes agents slower and developers miserable
  • Onboarding and offboarding run on tickets — leavers linger for weeks

The Cato solution

ZTNA, RBI and DLP — delivered from the nearest PoP

Cato treats the offshore estate as two device classes under one policy engine. Managed contractor laptops run the Cato Client and must pass a device-posture check before — and throughout — every session. Outsourcer-owned and personal devices get a clientless browser portal publishing only the applications that group is entitled to, with RBI rendering risky or unmanaged sessions remotely so the endpoint receives pixels, not data. CASB and DLP then keep source code and customer PII inside approved applications, blocking uploads to personal storage inline.

Crucially, all of it runs at the nearest PoP — Johannesburg for a South African delivery centre, Sydney for an Australian one — with the global private backbone carrying VDI, CRM and repository traffic to the region where the apps are hosted. That removes the long-haul public-internet leg that made legacy VDI painful at distance.

What each capability contributes

🪪

ZTNA for both device classes

Cato Client with continuous device-posture checks for managed contractor laptops; a clientless portal publishing only entitled apps for outsourcer-owned and personal devices. One policy engine for both.

🖥️

RBI keeps sessions on-screen only

Risky or unmanaged sessions render in an isolated remote browser — the endpoint receives pixels, not data, and copy, paste, upload and download can be restricted per application.

🔒

CASB & DLP around code and PII

Source code, secrets and customer records stay confined to approved applications; uploads to personal cloud storage and webmail are blocked inline, with the evidence logged.

🌍

Nearest PoP + private backbone

Agents onramp at Johannesburg or Sydney and ride the backbone to wherever VDI and CRM are hosted — predictable performance instead of long-haul public internet.

⚡

Churn absorbed by the directory

Group-based policy from the IdP: joiners inherit access on day one through group membership; leavers lose client and portal access with a single directory change.

🧾

Evidence for customers and regulators

Every session and every block attributed to a named user in one event store — the audit trail to hand over when the end customer's due-diligence team comes asking.

Sector blueprint

Typical offshore/BPO policies — a starter rulebase

Most offshore and BPO engagements converge on the same handful of controls: developers reach code through published app segments and nothing else, agents live inside one CRM on isolated sessions, source code and customer PII never leave approved apps, and group membership from the IdP does the joining and the leaving. The starter set below turns that shape into a small, readable rulebase you can adapt on the whiteboard with a prospect.

An illustrative starter blueprint — not a console export

Two honesty notes on rows 7–8. Rather than assume a native time-of-day scheduler, treat the shift window (row 7) as an operational practice — enable or disable the group's access around the contracted hours, scripted through the API if you want it automated — and confirm the exact mechanism with Cato PS for the tenant in question. The delivery-country control (row 8) is source-country awareness in the Client Connectivity Policy, restricting where users may connect from; it is geographic gating of the connection, not a legal guarantee of the user's location.

BPO scenarios

  • New engagement ramp-up. A partner needs hundreds of agents live within a week. Provision the BPO-Agents group in the IdP, sync it via SCIM, and every joiner inherits rows 4–8 on first login — no per-user tickets, no re-touching the rulebase as the headcount climbs.
  • Client audit of the offshore floor. The end customer's due-diligence team asks who touched their data. One event store answers it: every CRM session, every blocked upload and download, attributed to a named user and device — the evidence handed over without stitching logs from concentrators, VDI brokers and firewalls.
  • Contract end. When an engagement closes, disabling or emptying the group revokes rows 1–8 for the whole cohort in one move, and the retained event history is the bulk-revocation evidence — proof the access is gone and a record of what it ever reached.
  • Seat-vendor change. A partner swaps unmanaged seats for a managed build. The same users move from the clientless/RBI path (rows 4–5) to Cato Client with a passing posture profile, unlocking fuller access — a group and posture change, not a rebuild of the policy.
