Firewall Refresh to FWaaS: Animated Phased Journey

A firewall refresh usually buys the same posture on newer tin — and puts the next end-of-life date straight back on the calendar. This page is the animated companion to the full firewall-refresh use case: press play and watch a hardware renewal become an exit, from the first branch socket running FWaaS in monitor mode, through wave-by-wave cutover and policy convergence, to the day the renewal quote is cancelled.

Business objective

Refresh once more, or never again

End-of-support notices force a decision anyway — the only question is whether the answer is another sizing exercise or the last one. A phased exit de-risks the choice: FWaaS runs in monitor mode alongside each appliance before it enforces anything, so every cutover is validated against live traffic and rollback is a policy toggle, not a site visit. The walkthrough below steps through the five phases; for the full argument, economics and demo runbook see Firewall Refresh to FWaaS.

The walkthrough

The last refresh, phase by phase

Three branches — Bristol, Glasgow and Madrid — plus the London DC each run their own ageing firewall, every box tied down to the vendor's support and renewal cycle in the lower band. The upper band is the Cato backbone, where FWaaS lives. Watch each site's enforcement swing up to the cloud — amber while FWaaS only observes, green once it enforces — until the renewal is cancelled. At each phase

What changes, and what to notice

PhaseWhat changesWhat to notice
0 · Appliance estate Baseline — every site runs its own ageing firewall, all tied to the vendor's support and renewal cycle. Four separately-managed rulebases have drifted apart; the renewal quote forces a decision either way.
1 · First socket Bristol gets a socket; its rules are imported and FWaaS runs in monitor mode alongside the appliance. Nothing is enforced from the cloud yet — the amber lane is FWaaS watching live traffic to validate the policy.
2 · Branch waves Bristol and Glasgow cut enforcement over to FWaaS; Madrid starts its monitor run. Per-box policies converge into one CMA rulebase as each wave lands; dormant support ties are the rollback path.
3 · DC absorbed Madrid cuts over and the DC pair's functions move — internet/WAN policy to FWaaS, east-west segmentation to LAN Firewall. The last appliances sit powered-on but idle: a standby safety net, no longer in the traffic path.
4 · Renewal cancelled The soak period ends; the support renewal is cancelled and the hardware is removed. One global policy for every site, enforced in the PoP — nothing left to size, patch or EOL.
Why monitor-first takes the fear out of it

Every appliance keeps enforcing until its site's FWaaS policy has been proven against real traffic — the events tell you exactly what the cloud rulebase would have allowed or blocked before it decides anything. Cutover is then a policy change, and rollback is the same change in reverse; the appliance is still racked and licensed until the wave is signed off. The choreography of waves, co-existence and rollback design is the Migration Methodology page; the appliance economics and the walkthrough are on Firewall Refresh to FWaaS.
