Versa Networks (SD-WAN & SASE) to Cato

A Versa estate pairs a capable VOS edge with a head-end — Director, Controller, Analytics, often Concerto — that someone must host, size, patch and upgrade; in many accounts that someone is a carrier, and every change rides a provider ticket. This page maps each Versa component to its Cato equivalent and walks a five-phase path — early config export, eBGP hub co-existence with the MP-BGP overlay left intact, regional cutover waves with per-spoke rollback — sequenced around the provider contract where the SD-WAN is carrier-delivered.

Business objective

Two decision points: who runs the head-end, and who owns the contract

Versa's architecture is honest about where the work lives. VOS on a CSG appliance, certified white box or VM is the branch edge — SD-WAN routing plus an on-box NGFW/UTM stack. Behind the edges sits real server infrastructure: Versa Director (management, templates, workflows) in active-standby pairs, Versa Controller — a multi-instance MP-BGP route reflector distributing routes and IPsec security associations to every branch — in active-active pairs, a Versa Analytics cluster, and often Concerto as the orchestration portal on top. All components must run the same Versa software version, so upgrades are coordinated fleet events; the published requirements list Ubuntu 18.04 as the supported base OS for on-premises head-ends. Versa now sells a hosted-and-managed head-end service (VHM) — tacit acknowledgement that this is a burden many customers do not want.

The second decision point is commercial. Versa sells heavily through service providers — Verizon's managed SD-Branch and Lumen's SD-WAN and SASE services are built on Versa — and in those estates the provider owns the Director tenancy, often the CPE and sometimes the underlay circuits. The enterprise raises change tickets rather than making changes, and the network architecture is bound to the carrier contract term. Contract renewal, a dissatisfaction window, or Versa's own VersaONE re-platforming conversation (announced November 2024) are the natural moments to evaluate an alternative.

The objective of this migration: Sockets on the WAN edge, the global private backbone as the middle mile, single-pass inspection at the PoP, and one console — the Cato Management Application — with no head-end anywhere in the customer's or provider's estate. The control plane, the analytics store and the patching cycle become the vendor's problem, permanently.

The security record matters here not as FUD but as evidence of what self- or provider-hosted management infrastructure costs. State the facts accurately — patches exist for everything below — and let the operating model make the argument:

Director zero-day 2024

CVE-2024-39717, a file-upload flaw in Director's GUI, was exploited as a zero-day from at least June 2024 and added to the CISA KEV catalogue in August 2024. Black Lotus Labs attributed the campaign with moderate confidence to Volt Typhoon, which planted the in-memory “VersaMem” web shell on Director servers to harvest credentials — with victims in the ISP, MSP and IT sectors: the providers running Director for downstream enterprises. Fixed in Director 22.1.4+.

Concerto disclosures 2025

In May 2025, after a 90-day disclosure window, ProjectDiscovery published three Concerto vulnerabilities: CVE-2025-34027 (CVSS 10.0 — authentication bypass to file upload and RCE), CVE-2025-34026 (CVSS 9.2 — reverse-proxy Actuator bypass exposing heap dumps and session tokens) and CVE-2025-34025 (privileged container escape). Versa states hotfixes were available from March 2025 and a remediated release from 16 April 2025.

The provider-delivered model

Where the SD-WAN is carrier-managed (Verizon, Lumen and others), the enterprise typically cannot touch Director at all: config exports, policy changes and spoke operations all go through provider change tickets on provider timescales — and the patch cadence for the head-end components above is the provider's, not the customer's, decision. Cato has publicly positioned for enterprises opting out of carrier SD-WAN.

Ask yourself

“Who patched your Director, Controller, Analytics and Concerto in August 2024 and April 2025 — you, your carrier or Versa — and how long did it take? And if you asked for a full config export today, is that a console click or a change ticket with a lead time?”

What maps to what

Component-by-component mapping

The conversion is intent recovery, not lift-and-shift: Versa's template layers exist to keep hundreds of VOS instances consistent, and that whole problem class disappears when policy is expressed once in a cloud rulebase. What remains — site onboarding, policy-as-code — maps to the Cato GraphQL API and Terraform provider.

The migration path

Five phases from Director-managed to one platform

Provenance — read before you present

This page is authored from vendor documentation and Cato PS methodology research; it is not a delivery-verified runbook. Review the design — especially the eBGP handoff, filtering and rollback details, and anything contract-dependent — with Cato Professional Services before committing it to a customer plan.

The arc follows the Cato PS four-phase methodology (Discover & Design → Pilot, Build & Initial Rollout → Advanced Security & Broader Rollout → Tune, Optimise & Handover; the reference plan is four months), with co-existence broken out below because it is the load-bearing step in a Versa estate.

  1. Phase 1 — Discover & design: start the Director export early

    Export per-appliance configuration from Director (Appliances → Export Configuration, or show configuration | display set from the VOS CLI) and the staging/post-staging and device templates that generate branch config. Pull Analytics traffic data to build the real traffic matrix — Controller-distributed branch-to-branch IPsec SAs mean spoke-to-spoke flows rarely appear in design documents. In carrier-managed estates, raise the export request in week one: config, templates and Analytics data all arrive via the provider's change process, with lead time — and capture what the contract obliges the provider to hand over. In parallel, inventory contract end dates, notice periods, early-exit terms and which assets (CPE, circuits) are provider-owned.

    Clean the rule base before translating it: retire dead rules using Analytics hit data, collapse duplicate objects, flag any-any debt. Classify the estate — regional VOS hubs, DCs, branch classes (paired-CSG HA vs single, LTE backup), tenants, inbound NAT/published services, uCPE sites hosting VNFs, Titan vs Director-managed populations. Then fix the co-existence design: eBGP hub handoff pattern, AS numbering (the Cato side is eBGP-only; default Cato ASN 64515), route filtering and summarisation, and a cutover template per site class. Stand up the foundation: CMA baseline, IdP/SCIM, Client packaging and TLS-inspection certificates, socket logistics, SIEM planning.

  2. Phase 2 — Co-existence: parallel Sockets at the Versa hubs (PS Option F)

    Network → Sites

    Stand up Sockets (or vSockets) at the data centres and at each Versa hub/gateway site, alongside the existing kit; establish eBGP handoffs into each hub's LAN-side routing, and validate route exchange, symmetry and failover before any branch moves. Path symmetry is maintained by AS-path length: cross-region routes carry an extra inter-hub AS hop, so best-path selection anchors traffic to the local regional hub — exiting and returning through the same stateful VOS firewall. The Versa overlay's MP-BGP world stays intact and segmented on its side of the handoff; the two domains meet only at eBGP. Filter the default route and the prefixes of any half-migrated site at every handoff, and use BGP summaries and Cato's BGP filtering to keep the exchange tidy.

  3. Phase 3 — Pilot branch and pilot cohort, monitor-then-block

    Security → Internet Firewall

    Convert security policy from the cleaned rule base: WAN Firewall for site-to-site, the Internet Firewall wizard for the initial egress policy, IPS and NGAM in monitor mode first, and network rules with bandwidth profiles recreating Versa's steering intent (voice, business-critical, bulk). Cut over one representative branch with the chosen pattern and soak it. Deploy the Cato Client to a pilot cohort — Client Connectivity Policy in monitor mode first — and validate access, performance and UX. Keep one VPN/ZTNA data path active per endpoint during the overlap; rollback for the pilot is the standard spoke rollback below. Promote monitor rules to block only on evidence from real events.

  4. Phase 4 — Regional waves, sequenced with the provider

    Access → Client Connectivity Policy

    Migrate branches region by region behind their regional hub handoff: simple single-WAN branches first, then HA branches, then complex sites (uCPE/VNF hosts, local inbound services), hubs last. In provider-managed estates every wave's spoke-disable is a provider change ticket with a pre-agreed rollback window — the provider is in the loop for every cutover. Users follow as VSAC → Cato Client cohorts (the PS reference plan uses four waves): disable VSAC connectivity per cohort, enable the Cato Client, re-model access as ZTNA policy with Device Posture profiles. Enable TLS inspection in phases by URL category and pilot group. Keep provider circuits alive until the sites they serve are validated on Cato — and where the last mile is bundled with the managed SD-WAN, re-procure internet access per site ahead of its wave, because circuit delivery, not configuration, is usually the true critical path.

  5. Phase 5 — Optimise, decommission, and exit the contract cleanly

    Monitor → Events

    Tune QoS and policy from analytics, complete final site and user migrations, and bring the SIEM integration live. Then decommission in order: remove eBGP handoffs as each region completes → retire the VOS hubs → decommission the head-end (Director, Controller, Analytics, Concerto servers/VMs) once logging-retention obligations are met — having run CMA analytics in parallel since the pilot so trending exists before the Analytics cluster goes dark. Finally, return provider-owned CPE and serve contract notice per the terms mapped in Phase 1, and close with documentation, admin training and operational handover.

Co-existence & rollback

Two overlays, one eBGP seam

Asymmetry is the enemy. With two overlays joined in more than one place, a flow can exit through one region's hub and return through another — breaking stateful inspection on the VOS firewalls. The PS multi-region pattern (Option F, demonstrated against a FortiGate estate and applied unchanged here — the legacy hub only needs to speak eBGP) puts a parallel Socket at each Versa hub with an eBGP handoff, and lets AS-path length anchor each region's traffic to its local hub. Spoke migration is then a routing event: 1) disable the Versa spoke (via Director, or a provider change ticket) so its prefixes are withdrawn from the overlay and the eBGP exchange; 2) bring up the Cato socket site; 3) routes propagate automatically to both domains; 4) rollback is the mirror image — down the socket site, re-enable the Versa spoke, and BGP reconverges.

🧭

Symmetric by construction

AS-path anchoring makes each side prefer its local regional hub — no cross-region return paths through a different stateful VOS firewall.

↩️

Reversible per spoke

Disable the Versa spoke and its prefixes withdraw from both domains automatically; re-enable it and BGP reconverges. No per-site route engineering.

🧱

The overlay is left intact

The Controller-distributed MP-BGP world keeps running, segmented on its side of the handoff — un-migrated branches never notice the migration.

Gradual Deployment handles private (RFC1918) destinations only.

If the provider won't open BGP

Common in managed estates: no routing session into the provider's VOS world. The PS static alternative is an interconnect site with routed ranges — pre-stage real subnets on the interconnect and dummy ranges on the future Cato sites; at cutover delete the routed range and update the dummy range; rollback is re-adding the statics. Proven at scale in a ~300-site deployment.

Users and inbound services in transition

Run VSAC and the Cato Client side by side only in a controlled per-cohort overlap, with one VPN/ZTNA data path active at a time. Published/inbound services on VOS CPEs migrate deliberately — re-publish via Cato (remote port forwarding or static-IP egress) or leave them on a retained leg until their own change window.

Gotchas & objections

What bites, and what they'll say

“Our whole branch config is generated from Director templates”

True — and it cuts both ways. The effective config of any branch is the product of workflows, staging and post-staging templates, device templates and per-device bind data. Treat migration as intent recovery: export the templates and per-appliance configs, identify what actually varies per site (the bind data), and express the common intent once in CMA's single rulebase. The automation that keeps hundreds of VOS instances consistent solves a problem class that disappears with one cloud rulebase; what remains maps to the GraphQL API and Terraform provider.

“The provider runs it — we can't even export the config”

Common in carrier-managed estates: the enterprise has a read-only portal, not Director admin. Mitigations: contractual data-handover clauses, provider change tickets for exports, and reconstruction from customer-side artefacts (LAN addressing, firewall change records, the Analytics reports the customer can see). Budget discovery time accordingly, sequence waves around provider SLAs — and agree Analytics retention and export format in Phase 1, because leaving the provider's tenant forfeits historical reporting unless it is exported first.

Service chaining and uCPE VNFs

CSG platforms can host third-party VNFs service-chained with VOS. Every chained function needs an explicit disposition: absorbed into Cato's PoP inspection, replaced by a dedicated device, or retained on separate hardware. Miss one in discovery and a branch cutover strands a function nobody documented — inventory uCPE sites explicitly in Phase 1 and validate their disposition in the pilot.

Titan estates are a different migration

Titan is Versa's cloud-managed lean-IT tier — no customer head-end, so the migration is lighter (CPE swap, policy re-model). But the config detail available for export is shallower, and the estate was chosen precisely because the customer is lean-IT: plan for Cato PS or partner-led delivery rather than customer self-service.

“Versa is single-pass and single-OS too”

Versa's single-pass claim applies to VOS on the appliance and to its cloud gateways — but the architecture still requires someone (customer, carrier or Versa) to host and operate Director, Controller, Analytics and Concerto, and VersaONE is explicitly offered self-managed, co-managed or fully managed. The counter is not the packet path but the operating model: one cloud service, one console, no head-end anywhere in the estate, and the vendor — not the customer — patches the platform.

“We'll just patch Director and stay”

Be accurate: fixes shipped (Director 22.1.4+; a remediated Concerto release in April 2025). But CVE-2024-39717 was exploited as a zero-day against the management plane itself, by an actor targeting the ISP/MSP layer that operates Director for downstream enterprises — and VersaMem harvested credentials in memory, so patching alone did not settle the incident-response question. The Concerto disclosures then hit the orchestration layer at CVSS 9.2–10.0. The pattern argument: every self- or provider-hosted management component is attack surface the customer inherits.

Standard PoV hygiene

Niche VOS behaviours — bespoke NAT constructs, per-tenant routing instances, CGNAT, local-breakout exceptions, voice SLA-steering specifics — should be listed in discovery and validated in the PoV rather than assumed. Put them on the test plan by name.
