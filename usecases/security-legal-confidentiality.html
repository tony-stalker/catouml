Law Firms: Client Confidentiality & Ethical Walls

For a law firm, confidentiality is not a compliance line item — it is the product. Clients impose Outside Counsel Guidelines with security requirements and audit rights, conflicts demand ethical walls that actually hold, and lateral hires arrive and leave with matter access that must change the same day. This use case shows how one converged platform turns those obligations into enforceable, evidenced network policy: identity-based walls, DLP over privileged material, and an audit trail ready for the next client security questionnaire.

Business objective

Why this matters

A law firm sells two things: judgment and discretion. Privilege and confidentiality are existential — a single failure can lose a client, an injunction, or the firm's standing to act. In England and Wales the obligation is regulatory as well as commercial: the SRA Codes of Conduct (paragraph 6.3) require solicitors and firms to keep the affairs of current and former clients confidential, and paragraph 6.5 bars acting where a client's interest is adverse to another current or former client for whom the firm holds material confidential information — unless effective measures protect that information or informed consent is obtained. The courts set the bar for "effective" high: since Bolkiah v KPMG, an information barrier must leave no real risk of disclosure, and a real risk need not be substantial.

Commercial pressure now arrives faster than regulation. Sophisticated clients — banks, insurers, private equity — attach Outside Counsel Guidelines (OCGs) to their instructions: encryption standards, access-control expectations, breach-notification windows, restrictions on where matter data may live, and audit rights over the firm's security. Every panel review and new instruction brings another security questionnaire. The objective for the firm's COLP, IT director and general counsel is the same one, stated three ways: enforce confidentiality technically, not just contractually — and be able to prove it on demand.

The problem

Policy on paper, flat network in practice

Consider a fictional 400-lawyer, UK-headquartered firm with offices in London, Manchester and Dubai. Its confidentiality obligations are impeccably documented — and its estate undermines them four ways:

OCG sprawl and questionnaire fatigue

Dozens of clients, dozens of OCG variants, each with its own security schedule and audit rights. Every panel renewal means another questionnaire — and the answers live across firewall configs, VPN concentrators, DMS permissions and spreadsheets. Assembling evidence is a project each time, and partners feel it as lost fee-earning days.

Ethical walls that stop at the DMS

Walls are declared in the conflicts system and enforced in the document management system (iManage- or NetDocuments-class platforms). But the network underneath is flat: both matter teams sit on the same LAN and VPN, and nothing at the network layer distinguishes them. "Effective measures" with no real risk of disclosure is a hard claim when segregation exists in one application only.

Lateral churn on a same-day clock

Lateral hires arrive mid-matter and need access granted precisely — often behind a wall from day one because of prior-firm conflicts. Departures are worse: a leaver heading to opposing counsel must lose matter access the same day, and the firm must be able to show when it was revoked, not assert that it was.

Fee-earners work from everywhere

Courts, client sites, chambers, home, trains. Privileged material moves with them — on laptops, over hotel Wi-Fi, into personal cloud drives when deadlines bite. A VPN that drops users onto the flat network extends every weakness above to every location a fee-earner opens a lid.

Questions worth asking

"How is an ethical wall enforced today below the DMS — what stops a fee-earner on Matter A reaching Matter B's workspace over the network?" · "How long does a client security questionnaire take to answer, and how many systems hold the evidence?" · "When a lateral leaves for opposing counsel, who proves — with timestamps — that access was revoked?"

The Cato solution

Ethical walls as network policy — enforced and evidenced

Cato makes the ethical wall a property of the network itself. Matter teams are represented as IdP groups, and a pair of WAN Firewall rules per wall — allow each team to its own matter's application segment, everything else falling to the implicit deny — enforces the barrier on every flow, wherever it originates. A fee-earner in the office, at court or at home crosses the same PoP-enforced policy, because the Cato Client applies identical rules to remote users. DLP inspects what leaves — blocking privileged material bound for personal cloud storage — and every decision, allow or block, lands in the Cato Management Application as the evidence a client auditor asks for.

Position with care

Cato enforces the wall at the network layer — it complements the DMS's own ethical-wall and matter-security features (in iManage- or NetDocuments-class systems), it does not replace them, and no product integration is implied. Whether the combined measures meet the SRA's "effective measures" bar is a judgment for the firm and its COLP. Position Cato as making that claim materially easier to defend: segregation enforced in two independent layers, with evidence.

A blueprint — deploy it monitor-first, and let the wall lead

These rules are illustrative. Stand them up in monitor / event-only first, watch the hit counts against real traffic, and only then flip the blocks to enforce — exactly as the walkthrough does. And note the direction of authority: the wall rows must mirror the firm's conflicts register, one symmetric rule pair per declared wall. Policy follows the ethical wall — the conflicts team and COLP decide who is walled from what; Cato enforces and evidences that decision on the network. It does not define the wall.

Law-firm scenarios

  • A new conflict wall, stood up the same day. A fresh instruction throws up a conflict the moment it lands. Once the conflicts team declares the wall, you add a single rule pair scoped to the two matter IdP groups (rows 1–2 and their mirror), deployed monitor-first then enforced — the barrier exists at the network layer within the hour, a second independent layer beneath the DMS's own wall, not a change ticket waiting in a queue.
  • A client's OCG security questionnaire arrives. Rather than a firm-wide scavenger hunt, the evidence pack comes from one console: the rulebase as configured (walls, DLP, access), events showing those controls firing on real traffic, and the Audit Trail of every change — the raw material for most questionnaire sections, assembled in an afternoon instead of costing partners fee-earning days.
  • A lateral partner exits to opposing counsel. Remove them from the matter groups in the IdP and SCIM collapses their wall and ZTNA scope the same day — rows 1–5 simply stop matching. The Audit Trail and events then give the timestamped who and when of the revocation, and the history of what that identity could reach: evidence of a clean exit, not an assertion of one.
  • A deal-room period on a live transaction. While the deal is open, tighten the controls for the deal-team group — move row 6's DLP Legal profile from monitor to block on that group's traffic and keep row 7's GenAI block in force — then relax back to baseline at completion. Because scoping is by group, the heightened posture lands on the deal team without disturbing the rest of the firm, and the change window is recorded in the Audit Trail.
