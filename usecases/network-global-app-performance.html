Global Application & UCaaS Performance

Voice, video and SaaS are unforgiving of latency and loss — and the public internet between continents delivers both, unpredictably, while global MPLS is unaffordable at that reach. This use case shows how traffic enters the nearest Cato PoP and rides the private backbone — accelerated, loss-protected and QoS-prioritised — so a user in Sydney gets the same application experience as one sitting next to the London DC.

Business objective

Why this matters

The applications the business feels most — Teams and Zoom calls, video meetings, M365 and the rest of the SaaS estate — are exactly the ones most sensitive to latency, jitter and packet loss. A file sync that runs 20 % slower is an annoyance; a choppy voice call with the customer is a reputation problem, escalated the same afternoon.

The objective: give every user, in every region, predictable performance for real-time and SaaS applications — without buying a global MPLS network to get it.

The problem

Distance is the enemy — and you don't own the middle

Within a metro, the internet is usually fine. Between continents it is a chain of providers you have no contract with, each making its own routing decisions. The traditional answer — international MPLS — solves predictability but at a price and provisioning timescale that rules it out for global reach, and it never covered SaaS or remote users anyway.

The public internet

  • Best-effort, hot-potato routing across many providers
  • Latency, jitter and loss vary hour to hour — worst on long haul
  • Peering congestion you can't see and can't escalate
  • No SLA — nobody owns your Sydney→London path end to end
  • TCP throughput collapses over high-latency, lossy paths

Global MPLS

  • Predictable — but unaffordable at intercontinental scale
  • Months to provision a new international circuit
  • No on-ramp for SaaS, cloud or remote users
  • QoS stops at the provider edge, per-region contracts
  • Capacity upgrades are slow, disruptive and expensive
Ask yourself

“When someone in Sydney says their Teams calls to London are choppy, what do you do today — and when the problem sits in the middle mile, between providers, who do you even call?”

The Cato solution

Nearest PoP in, private backbone across

Every site, user and cloud instance connects to its nearest Cato PoP. From there, traffic rides the global private backbone — PoPs interconnected across multiple Tier-1 carriers, with the optimal route chosen continuously and a 99.999 % uptime SLA between PoPs. Every PoP runs the full software stack, so TCP proxy acceleration, packet-loss mitigation and app- and identity-aware QoS are applied in the path, end to end. SaaS traffic uses smart egress: it exits the backbone at the PoP closest to the application instance, not back at head office.

What the PoPs do to the traffic

TCP proxy acceleration

The PoP terminates the TCP session locally and proxies it across the backbone, so window scaling isn't throttled by end-to-end round-trip time. Long-haul file transfers stop crawling.

Packet-loss mitigation

Loss on the path is recovered between PoPs rather than punished with end-to-end retransmits — the difference between a glitch nobody notices and a robotic voice call.

Smart egress for SaaS

SaaS traffic exits at the PoP closest to the application instance — a Sydney user reaches M365 via the front door nearest the tenant, not via a UK breakout.

What the customer gets

📞

Predictable UCaaS quality

Voice and video are classified and prioritised end to end — at the socket, at the ingress PoP and across the backbone — so calls survive the busy hour.

🚀

Faster long-haul transfers

TCP acceleration turns intercontinental throughput from a function of round-trip time into a function of bandwidth — replication and large file moves finish sooner.

🌍

No per-region tuning

One backbone, one policy. There are no regional appliances to tune, no WAN optimisers to maintain, no “APAC exception” configs to drift.

🪪

Per-app QoS tied to identity

Bandwidth priorities key on the application and the user — the exec's Zoom call and the payroll upload win over a background backup, per person, globally.
