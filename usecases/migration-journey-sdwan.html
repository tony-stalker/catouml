SD-WAN to Cato: Animated Phased Journey

SD-WAN displacements rarely stall on whether Cato is better — they stall on one question in the room: how do both fabrics coexist mid-cutover without breaking routing? This is that answer as a walkthrough. Press play and watch the eBGP-hub journey unfold phase by phase — a parallel socket at the hub, spoke-by-spoke cutover with instant rollback, then the legacy overlay switched off. Business objective

The question that stalls the deal

A prospect running a legacy SD-WAN fabric — the same overlay steering every branch back to a regional hub — believes the destination. What they can't picture is the transition: the weeks when half the estate is on Cato and half is still on the incumbent overlay, and traffic has to flow cleanly between them without asymmetric routing tearing sessions apart.

The proven answer is the eBGP-hub co-existence pattern: stand a Cato Socket up in parallel at the hub, hand routes between the two fabrics over eBGP, and let AS-path length anchor every site to its own hub so paths stay symmetric. Below, that pattern is animated end to end. It is deliberately generic — a regional hub, three spokes and a DC — so it maps onto any legacy SD-WAN overlay. The vendor-specific mechanics live in the Fortinet playbook and the routing options A–F in the Migration Methodology.

Phase by phase

What happens at each step

The same five phases, as a reference table — what changes on the ground, and what routing looks like while that phase is live.

PhaseWhat changesRouting during this phase
0 · Legacy overlay Baseline: every branch on the incumbent SD-WAN fabric, steering back to a central legacy hub. No Cato. All spoke-to-spoke and spoke-to-DC traffic transits the legacy hub over the overlay. Nothing to co-exist with yet.
1 · Parallel socket at hub A Cato Socket is stood up beside the legacy hub; the Cato PoP and backbone come online; an eBGP handoff peers the two fabrics. Legacy still carries all production. The fabrics exchange routes over eBGP — validated with test prefixes before a single site moves.
2 · First spoke cuts over Bristol's legacy spoke is disabled and a Cato site brought up; its overlay link falls dormant. Bristol rides the Cato backbone; to reach still-legacy sites it crosses the hub eBGP handoff. Rollback re-enables the legacy spoke.
3 · Spokes migrating Leeds cuts over next; Glasgow stays on the overlay. The legacy hub begins to empty out. Migrated spokes are symmetric on Cato, legacy spokes symmetric on the overlay — AS-path length anchors each to its own hub. No asymmetry across the handoff.
4 · Overlay retired Glasgow moves; the legacy hub, its overlay links, controllers and licences are decommissioned. Every site and the DC run on the Cato backbone. The eBGP handoff and the legacy overlay are removed — one fabric remains.
The routing insight

Why routing stays symmetric throughout

The whole journey hangs on one property: while both fabrics are live, a flow and its return must take the same hub — otherwise the stateful firewalls on each side see only half the conversation and sessions drop. Three things hold that line.

🔌

A parallel socket at the hub

The Cato Socket sits alongside the legacy hub at the same site — the two fabrics meet in one place, so there is a single, controlled boundary between them rather than a mesh of interconnects.

🔁

An eBGP handoff between the fabrics

Routes are exchanged dynamically at that boundary. Migrating a spoke withdraws its prefixes from one fabric and advertises them into the other automatically — no per-site static route engineering during the window.

🧭

AS-path length anchors each site

Crossing between fabrics adds an AS hop, so best-path selection keeps a site's traffic — and its return — on its own regional hub. Symmetry is a property of the routing, not a manual pin.

↩️

Rollback is reconvergence

Every cutover reverses by re-enabling configuration that still exists: re-enable the legacy spoke and BGP reconverges. No rebuild, no truck roll — which is exactly what makes the phased approach safe to start.
