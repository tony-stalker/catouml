Data Breach Investigation with DLP Forensics

A DLP event has fired — is it a genuine data leak or a false positive? Event metadata alone can't answer that; the actual matched data can. This use case shows DLP Forensics: encrypted evidence written to storage the customer owns, retrievable directly from the CMA, governed by RBAC and fully audited from configuration change to evidence view.

Business objective

Why this matters

Security teams need to determine whether a DLP event is a real data leak or a false positive — and when an incident is real, they need forensic evidence to determine its impact: what data left, how much, and who was involved.

The objective: seamless DLP incident investigation directly from the CMA, backed by evidence the organisation owns and controls — not a black-box verdict, and not a second copy of sensitive data sitting in someone else's storage.

The investigation gap

Real leak or false positive?

Metadata alone can't answer it

A typical DLP event tells you which rule matched, which user, which application. It does not show you the data that matched. Analysts are left guessing — so real leaks get dismissed as noise, and false positives trigger full incident-response fire drills.

Forensics without governance is a new risk

The evidence is the sensitive data. If matched content is captured into vendor-owned storage, readable by anyone with console access and with no audit of who viewed it, your DLP tool has just created a second copy of the leak.

Ask yourself

“When your DLP flags an upload as a credit-card leak, can your analysts see exactly what matched — and can you prove afterwards who looked at that evidence?”

The Cato solution

Customer-owned, encrypted, governed forensics

When a DLP rule fires, Cato writes an encrypted forensic snippet — up to and including the full file — into an S3 bucket in the customer's own AWS account. Evidence is encrypted with a per-account unique key, retrieval is governed by RBAC, and every related action — configuration change, evidence view, download — is fully audited. The analyst never leaves the CMA: the event, the incident detail and the evidence are one click apart.

The four guarantees

🔐

Forensics encryption

All forensic evidence is encrypted and stored using a per-account unique encryption key — evidence in your bucket is unreadable without your account's key.

🔑

RBAC-governed access

All forensics are governed by role-based access control. Only administrators with the right role can open the evidence — seeing the event doesn't mean seeing the data.

📋

Audit & activity logging

Every action related to DLP Forensics is fully audited — configuration changes, evidence views, downloads. You can prove exactly who saw what, and when.

🗄️

Customer-owned full-file storage

Evidence is written to an S3 bucket in your own AWS account, with full file storage supported for files up to 20 MB — you keep custody of the data.

In the CMA

Anatomy of a data incident

This is what the analyst sees. A user attempted to upload “Document with CC Information.doc” to Microsoft Copilot; the rule “Copilot restrict PII” matched the CC-UK DLP profile (credit-card numbers) and blocked the upload. The incident pane carries the full context — user, application, activity type, file, matched data types — and below it, the Forensics panel holds the secured evidence behind a single View Evidence button.

Why “Evidence is Secured” matters in the room

Point at the locked panel: the evidence exists, but even in a live demo it isn't casually exposed. Viewing it is a deliberate, role-gated, logged action — exactly the governance a DPO or compliance officer will ask about.
